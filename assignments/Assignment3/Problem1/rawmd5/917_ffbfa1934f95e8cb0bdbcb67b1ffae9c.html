From Dale Earnhardt's 'bastard' driving to his tough love with Dale Jr., the NASCAR star had no problem being the bad guy

BY Sherryl Connelly
NEW YORK DAILY NEWS
Saturday, February 6, 2016, 10:01 PM
  • A
  • A
  • A
  • facebook
  • Tweet
  • email
Share this URL

Dale Earnhardt drove NASCAR from its Southern good ol’ boy roots into a national obsession at 200 mph and a tankful of menace. He lived and died a legend.

His son, Dale Earnhardt Jr., took possession of that legend but his most notable contribution to the sport may ultimately lie in winning the Most Popular Driver award 13 seasons in a row. Even that isn’t a record.

Throw in a grieving widow turned recluse grasping for control of her husband’s profitable legacy and a daughter who could have been a contender and it’s a helluva of a story.

And the one told by Jay Busbee, a writer for Yahoo Sports, in “Earnhardt Nation: The Full-Throttle Saga of NASCAR’s First Family.” The new book arrives just days before the flag waves at the 58th Daytona 500, the season opener and the sport’s Super Bowl.

Dale Sr. followed his father Ralph into racing. Ralph was a brilliant short-track racer.

While it took Dale Sr. some time to become established on the track, he did win first pole at fatherhood. Earnhardt had his son, Kerry, at 17 before another failed marriage produced Kelley and Dale Jr.

By the time his impoverished ex-wife, Brenda, sent Kelly and Dale Jr., to live with him, Earnhardt was on his third marriage to Teresa, the daughter of a racing family, who it turned out had a real flair for what would come to be known as branding.

Together, the two of them, Busbee writes, would one day change the face of NASCAR.

It wasn’t until 1987 that Earnhardt dominated the sport, though. Till then, he’d won some championships, but that year he went full-Earnhardt. If a car could snarl, his would as he bullied his way through creating openings where there were none.

“If the difference between victory and failure meant knocking the living crap out the next guy, so be it,” writes Busbee.

His inner “bastard” came out, roaring at full speed.

But Earnhardt wasn’t just a thug on wheels. His driving skills were formidable. They said he could read the air as his Chevy flew through spaces it couldn’t possibly fit.

Those skills and that aggression were both on display at the 1987 All-Star race. Earnhardt was in the lead when Bill Elliot pulled up and with a tap sent Earnhardt sideways, shooting through the grassy infield.

Earnhardt should have at least spun out. Instead he created one of the most legendary moments in race history.

He never took his foot off the gas, keeping pace with Elliot until he was able to wrench his car back on the track, still in the lead. Fans simply couldn’t believe what they saw. The maneuver went down as “The Pass in the Grass,” though technically it wasn’t that.

Things only got worse for Elliot. Earnhardt maneuvered the other driver into a position from where he could run Elliot’s car high on the wall on the turn. Earnhardt won.

Elliot, whose sweet temper was almost his trademark, stormed into the press room, “I’m sick and tired of it ... If a man has to run over you to beat you, it’s time for this stuff to stop.”

Earnhardt merely shrugged and drawled, “Not everyone can be the good guy.”

To short-list Earnhardt’s lifetime achievements it need only be said he won seven championships. But the Man in Black’s daring feats drew such worship through the years that the buzz pulled in new, even Northern, fans just as NASCAR was positioning itself to go national.

The newly anointed but still rough-hewn celebrity didn’t always play along. While Earnhardt was right proud of his screen moment in Burt Reynolds’ “Stroker Ace,” when a Hollywood crew entered two cars in an official race to film footage for Tom Cruise’s “Days of Thunder,” Earnhardt threatened to wreck them.

He would have done it too. Being surly was his thing. While he was immensely charitable in private, particularly moved by the plight of a child in need, everywhere else he was an intimidating presence.

It was an essential ingredient in the Earnhardt persona that Teresa brilliantly marketed down to every last #3 keychain and Man in Black beer cooler. Between her sales and his winnings, their fortune swelled to $40 million.

Life was sweet. His workspace on the Mooresville, N.C. estate was so big and so well-appointed that it was dubbed the “garage-mahal.” Earnhardt owned a Lear jet, a helicopter and a 76-foot Hatteras.

The money didn’t seem to trickle down to his kids, though he did use his helicopter once to swoop low over the pool, rousting Junior’s hard-partying buddies. In early interviews, Junior talked about how much beer he could put away. It was a lot.

When Junior started racing, the boy was so underfunded some nights he slept in his car.

Dale Sr. wouldn't back Dale Jr.'s racing dreams financially until he saw that the kid had what it took to race for DEI.

Dale Sr. saw his daughter Kelley Earnhardt as being the one with real talent, and wooed her home from college to race, hiring Junior to help build a car for his sister.

But in the mid-90s, the NASCAR culture had no use for a woman driver, even one with the last name Earnhardt. At one point she couldn’t even hire a pit crew. Kelley called it quits and went over to the business side, ironically playing a major role in bringing Danica Patrick to NASCAR in 2010.

Earnhardt didn’t fully throw himself behind his son until Junior showed he had at least some of the right stuff racing for Dale Earnhardt Inc. One of Senior's first steps was to set up a licensing deal for his boy. It didn’t take long for Dale Earnhardt Jr. to become one of the most successful brands in sports history.

But the reconciled father and son didn’t have many years together.

The legend’s career was definitely in a backslide when in 1998, after 19 fails, Earnhardt finally won NASCAR’s biggest race, the Daytona 500. Pulling the black #3 onto pit road he was met by every crew member from every team lined up to shake his hand.

In 2001, the Daytona 500 had very different outcome.

As the race reached its final moments, Earnhardt was third behind his son and DEI driver Michael Waltrip. In the celebratory melee that greeted Waltrip’s victory, people started asking why the boss wasn’t there.

The accident that killed Earnhardt less than half a mile from the finish line hadn’t seemed that bad. Kenny Schrader piled into Earnhardt’s right side, sending him into the wall at 147 mph. There were many injuries, but it was a basilar skull fracture that killed him.

NASCAR had lost its greatest driver at its greatest race.

Junior considered quitting. Yet he was strapped in for the first race after his father’s funeral at Rockingham. On the first lap, an accident forced Junior into the wall and crushed his car. NASCAR nation had a very bad moment until Junior crawled out unscathed.

Junior’s life in the fast lane accelerated, just not on the track. MTV “Cribs” filmed at his home in his purple lit “Club E,” where he could cram in as many as 250 party-goers.

He made People’s 50 Most Beautiful list and showed up in a Jay Z music video, among others. He celebrated his 30th birthday with a legendary throwdown that included Ludacris and Third Eye Blind. Junior showed up at his next race with a black eye.

Still he was racing and racing a lot. Following an accident in 2004, he won four races immediately afterward — but in the next eight years he rode to victory only three times.

Meanwhile, Teresa’s control over DEI was total. She even owned the rights to Junior’s name. He managed to wrest that much back before things with his hauntingly reclusive and seemingly hostile stepmother came to a head.

Increasingly, people were referring to Teresa as an absentee owner who, as one observer put it, didn’t realize that while “she owned the team her stepson owned the sport.”

Junior split with DEI in 2007. His stepmother wouldn’t let him take #8 with him, so he switched up to 88.

During his long losing streak, Junior would retreat from sniping fans to his gated spread north of Mooresville.

One of the pleasure centers he installed at “Dirty Mo’ Acres” was a complete facsimile of an Old West town.The sheriff’s department had working jail cells and Silverado Saloon was fully functional.

Junior went on to better years and his share of high-profile wins, though his record doesn’t approach his father’s. Now in his 40s, emerging from his playboy years, he recently became engaged to interior designer Amy Reimman.

Kelley Earnhardt is a prominent business figure in NASCAR. Their much younger stepsister, Taylor Earnhardt Putnam, is a rodeo rider.

Teresa hasn’t been made a public appearance since Dale Earnhardt’s induction to the hall of fame in 2010. Devoted NASCAR fans resent how little she’s done with his legacy in the fifteen years since his death.

But even though his museum seems a barely attended shell these days, Busbee makes one thing abundantly clear.

Dale Earnhardt’s legend has never faded.

“Earnhardt Nation: The Full Throttle Saga of NASCAR's First Family,” by Jay Busbee, goes on sale Feb. 16.
