<h2 class='date-header'><span>Sunday, March 27, 2016</span></h2>

          <div class="date-posts">
        
<div class='post-outer'>
<div class='post hentry uncustomized-post-template' itemprop='blogPost' itemscope='itemscope' itemtype='http://schema.org/BlogPosting'>
<meta content='https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-o8hc27M6Smc/VvfgnFCSB7I/AAAAAAAAFRM/pZY1LGPYzGc39_HG3YGMADf-euzyY-jEg/s640/Fall.jpeg' itemprop='image_url'/>

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://intheframefilmreviews.blogspot.com/2016/03/ultimate-playlists-9-fall.html'>Ultimate Playlists 9: The Fall</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-3852681291089100960' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-o8hc27M6Smc/VvfgnFCSB7I/AAAAAAAAFRM/pZY1LGPYzGc39_HG3YGMADf-euzyY-jEg/s1600/Fall.jpeg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="384" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-o8hc27M6Smc/VvfgnFCSB7I/AAAAAAAAFRM/pZY1LGPYzGc39_HG3YGMADf-euzyY-jEg/s640/Fall.jpeg" width="640" /></a></div>
<span style="font-size: x-small;">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Image from TheGuardian.com</span><br />
<br />
<span style="font-size: small;">Have you ever been to a concert and wished the band had played certain 
songs? I know I have. The reality is, you'll never get exactly what you 
want. However, you are free to build any playlist you like. I have 
decided to write a series showcasing the Top 20 songs from some of my 
favorite bands.</span><br />
<br />
<span style="font-size: small;">For those of you who are old enough to remember cassette tapes, you'll 
know the terrible anguish of trying to fit the songs together. How 
annoying when the last song you picked was still playing as the tape ran
 out! Those days are over and it's now a simple task to throw together a
 playlist using your computer, iPod or a USB. I know that having USB 
capability has enhanced my driving pleasure because it's so easy to 
update a list of songs.</span><br />
<br />
<span style="font-size: small;">But there's also more to creating a playlist than simply deciding which 
songs to include. Like an actual concert, or even a single song, a good 
playlist features changes in mood and tempo. If a band opens with the 
three songs that the audience most wants to hear, the rest of the 
performance might fall flat. My own particular method of creating a 
playlist has a number of considerations:
</span>
<span style="font-size: small;"><br /></span>
<br />
<ul>
<li><span style="font-size: small;">Mix up the duration of the songs</span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: small;">Put space between songs from the same album, unless the two are better when played in sequence</span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: small;">Build to a natural high, slow it down again, and finish with a real flourish</span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: small;">Put in some newer songs close to the start, assuming there are newer songs worthy of inclusion</span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: small;">For bands with more than one vocalist, mix up the sequence depending on who is singing</span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: small;">Speed and style matters, so mix it up unless there is a good reason not to</span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: small;">Albums often have a great choice of opening and closing song that work best in that particular spot&nbsp;</span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: small;">The final three or four songs might resemble an encore if it was a real concert</span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: small;">Leave the listener satisfied and wanting more&nbsp;</span></li>
</ul>
<span style="font-size: small;">
<br />
Being obsessed with your favorite band is a little bit like being in love; you can see most of the flaws, but you start to see those idiosyncracies as reasons why you love that particular person. You might find yourself smiling when you notice them. I'm not saying that I was ever in love with Mark E. Smith, but The Fall hold a special place in my heart.</span><br />
<br />
<span style="font-size: small;">For one thing, the first concert I ever attended featured The Fall. The difference between hearing recorded music and feeling it vibrate through you at a live show is huge, so I'll never forget that musical awakening. I've been to hundreds of concerts over the years, but I've seen The Fall around 30 times; more than any other band. Between 1980 and 1992, I would have argued that they were the most important band on the planet. No band has ever been my absolute favorite for a longer period of time. The friends I saw most of those shows with were, and still are, very important to me. And, of course, The Fall were John Peel's favorite band and Peel was someone that I cared deeply about.</span><br />
<br />
<span style="font-size: small;">According to Wikipedia, The Fall have released 30 studio albums, 32 live albums, 5 albums that are a mix of live and studio songs, 40 compilations, 13 EPs and 46 singles. I'm not going to count, but that sounds close. The introduction of the CD format dramatically increased the running time of albums I had previously owned on vinyl. Some of the songs from this playlist were not available on the original albums.</span><br />
<br />
<span style="font-size: small;">My introduction to the band came courtesy of the John Peel show. The love affair began slowly and it took me a while to fully absorb Smith's shouts, shrieks and pterodactyl cackles as something I wanted to listen to. There's no question that Smith's unique vocals are what define the band's sound even now, but the songs that I care about the most only reached those heights because of the incredible rhythm section.&nbsp;</span><br />
<br />
<span style="font-size: small;">Smith changes musicians like women change shoes, but for the duration of my obsession, a core group was present, like a favorite pair of sneakers that are too comfortable to part with. Stephen Hanley's bass was a huge part of the sound; Marc Riley, Craig Scanlon and Martin Bramah also helped define that classic guitar sound, while Karl Burns, Paul Hanley and (later) Simon Wolstencroft often provided the band with two drummers during concerts. The combination of those band members gave Smith an incredible platform from which to bellow or mumble his drunken vocals.</span><br />
<br />
<span style="font-size: small;">I consider <i>Cerebral Caustic</i> (1995) the last great Fall album. After <i>Levitate</i> (1997), Smith eventually fired the band and rebuilt it with new personnel, except for Julia Nagle (keyboards). My love affair was over. Many will argue that The Fall are still producing great music, but something died for me when that incredible engine room was removed from the equation. That will help explain my upcoming choices.</span><br />
<br />
<span style="font-size: small;">Before I reveal my final 20 songs for the playlist, I want to emphasize how difficult it was to cut so many great songs. For almost 20 years, The Fall produced important music filled with energy and venom. The ever-changing lineup always managed to sound tight. The only real wildcard was Smith's vocals. If you think his lyrics are hard to make out, try listening to him speak!</span><br />
<br />
<span style="font-size: small;">My favorite Fall album is definitely <i>Grotesque</i>. Almost every song from it just missed my final list, but I think of it as the best example of The Fall's definitive sound. It includes short rants, meandering rants, great riffs, a couple of compelling stories and a lot of humor. Smith's dark sense of humor is present in the lyrics on every album. If you have never heard the band before, you might wonder why Smith ends every line with -ah. I don't have a clue, but you have to admit that his vocals are distinctive. This is a band that probably won't appeal to new listeners on just one playing. The songs are often repetitive and gradually burrow their way into your brain. If you give the music a chance, you might start to go through some of the things I first felt more than 30 years ago.</span><br />
<br />
<span style="font-size: small;">So here it is. The result of the almost impossible task to reduce The Fall's output to their 20 best songs. As usual, my <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1cVegCDlbkA&amp;list=PLBPVVpmmaxHl4fngQU-qA8XN1UQnhhidh">YouTube playlist</a> is available:</span><br />
<br />
<span style="font-size: small;">Lay of the Land<br />The Man Whose Head Expanded<br />Kicker Conspiracy<br />Eat Y'Self Fitter<br />Lie Dream of a Casino Soul<br />Guest Informant<br />I'm Into C.B.<br />Prole Art Threat<br />Totally Wired<br />Athlete Cured <br />How I Wrote Elastic Man<br />No Xmas for John Quays<br />The Classical<br />Dead Beat Descendant<br />Hey Student<br />Cruiser's Creek<br />New Face in Hell<br />Paranoid Man in Cheap Shit Room<br />Free Range<br />Bremen Nacht (Alternative)</span><br />

<br />
Have you ever been to a concert and wished the band had played certain 
songs? I know I have. The reality is, you'll never get exactly what you 
want. However, you are free to build any playlist you like. I have 
decided to write a series showcasing the Top 20 songs from some of my 
favorite bands.<br />
<br />
For those of you who are old enough to remember cassette tapes, you'll 
know the terrible anguish of trying to fit the songs together. How 
annoying when the last song you picked was still playing as the tape ran
 out! Those days are over and it's now a simple task to throw together a
 playlist using your computer, iPod or a USB. I know that having USB 
capability has enhanced my driving pleasure because it's so easy to 
update a list of songs.<br />
<br />
But there's also more to creating a playlist than simply deciding which 
songs to include. Like an actual concert, or even a single song, a good 
playlist features changes in mood and tempo. If a band opens with the 
three songs that the audience most wants to hear, the rest of the 
performance might fall flat. My own particular method of creating a 
playlist has a number of considerations:
<br />
<br />
<ul>
<li>Mix up the duration of the songs</li>
<li>Put space between songs from the same album, unless the two are better when played in sequence</li>
<li>Build to a natural high, slow it down again, and finish with a real flourish</li>
<li>Put in some newer songs close to the start, assuming there are newer songs worthy of inclusion</li>
<li>For bands with more than one vocalist, mix up the sequence depending on who is singing</li>
<li>Speed and style matters, so mix it up unless there is a good reason not to</li>
<li>Albums often have a great choice of opening and closing song that work best in that particular spot&nbsp;</li>
<li>The final three or four songs might resemble an encore if it was a real concert</li>
<li>Leave the listener satisfied and wanting more&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<br />
Has there ever been a musician that has changed identity more often or more successfully than David Bowie? Instead of my usual concert photo, I've borrowed the above album collage from TheSource.com to illustrate those many phases. Even if you are not a fan, you have to admit that it's pretty cool. Bowie remained original up to and including his final album release. <br />
<br />
I was wary of featuring David Bowie in this Ultimate Playlist series because of the flood of material that appeared following his death in January. However, he has impacted my life in a lot of ways and I didn't want to omit him.<br />
<br />
When I was a kid, with my musical tastes dominated by glam, novelty records, and all manner of embarrassing things, Bowie's music captured my attention like nothing ever had. Hey, I was 12 or 13, so give me a break! I remember playing <i>Hunky Dory</i> and <i>Aladdin Sane</i> at my neighbor's house after buying them from a store my grandfather ran. I credit Bowie with being the first 'proper' artist I ever became obsessed with.<br />
<br />
My grandmother was curious about my obsession and made an attempt to understand it. I have fond memories of her reading the lyrics to <i>Hunky Dory</i>. I have a sneaking suspicion that she imagined that <i>Kooks</i> could have been written about her and my grandfather.<br />
<br />
I distinctly remember anticipating the release of <i>Low</i>. It was the first time I was ahead of the curve and not just reacting to existing music. When it finally arrived, I wondered why Bowie had included so many instrumental synthesizer tracks on the second side - a trend that continued with the release of <i>Heroes - </i>but I adjusted and regard those albums as two of his best releases.<br />
<br />

<div class="MsoNoSpacing">
<span lang="EN-US">I am sure that there will be many readers who will look at the above list and at my final 20 and think that I have omitted some of the best songs Bowie ever produced. That's how strong his catalogue is. But I hope that you will at least respect my choices, even if you are outraged at some of the omissions.</span></div>
<div class="MsoNoSpacing">
<br /></div>
<div class="MsoNoSpacing">
<span lang="EN-US">As for favorite albums, I would mention <i>The Man Who Sold the World</i>, <i>Heroes</i>, <i>Hunky Dory</i>, <i>Low</i> and <i>Outside</i>. That's not to say that most of the others don't contain songs that you simply need to hear. I'm sure a lot of fans would put <i>Ziggy Stardust</i> and <i>Aladdin Sane</i> close to the top of their list, while others would choose <i>Let's Dance</i> or <i>Young Americans</i>. My closest friend would probably ask me where the hell <i>Earthling</i> was and why I haven't included anything from that. One of the biggest surprises I had regarding Bowie was listening to <i>Outside</i>. It was such an unexpected return to the sound I adored, but also updated in a weird and wonderful kind of way. I must have seen 10 concerts on that tour.</span></div>

<div class='post hentry uncustomized-post-template' itemprop='blogPost' itemscope='itemscope' itemtype='http://schema.org/BlogPosting'>

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://intheframefilmreviews.blogspot.com/2016/03/ultimate-playlists-7-television.html'>Ultimate Playlists 7: Television</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-469660649929164168' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-BCVVIUD-EzY/Vu9PxDyzlNI/AAAAAAAAFQo/I9lWMieHj6EP1zUKmyTckHUymgirSisfA/s1600/television.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="386" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-BCVVIUD-EzY/Vu9PxDyzlNI/AAAAAAAAFQo/I9lWMieHj6EP1zUKmyTckHUymgirSisfA/s640/television.jpg" width="640" /></a></div>
<br />
Have you ever been to a concert and wished the band had played certain 
songs? I know I have. The reality is, you'll never get exactly what you 
want. However, you are free to build any playlist you like. I have 
decided to write a series showcasing the Top 20 songs from some of my 
favorite bands.<br />
<br />
For those of you who are old enough to remember cassette tapes, you'll 
know the terrible anguish of trying to fit the songs together. How 
annoying when the last song you picked was still playing as the tape ran
 out! Those days are over and it's now a simple task to throw together a
 playlist using your computer, iPod or a USB. I know that having USB 
capability has enhanced my driving pleasure because it's so easy to 
update a list of songs.<br />
<br />
But there's also more to creating a playlist than simply deciding which 
songs to include. Like an actual concert, or even a single song, a good 
playlist features changes in mood and tempo. If a band opens with the 
three songs that the audience most wants to hear, the rest of the 
performance might fall flat. My own particular method of creating a 
playlist has a number of considerations:
<br />
<br />
<br />
<ul>
<li>Mix up the duration of the songs</li>
<li>Put space between songs from the same album, unless the two are better when played in sequence</li>
<li>Build to a natural high, slow it down again, and finish with a real flourish</li>
<li>Put in some newer songs close to the start, assuming there are newer songs worthy of inclusion</li>
<li>For bands with more than one vocalist, mix up the sequence depending on who is singing</li>
<li>Speed and style matters, so mix it up unless there is a good reason not to</li>
<li>Albums often have a great choice of opening and closing song that work best in that particular spot&nbsp;</li>
<li>The final three or four songs might resemble an encore if it was a real concert</li>
<li>Leave the listener satisfied and wanting more&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<br />
Television were responsible for the best album of the seventies and most of the planet doesn't have a clue what their music sounds like. That's why I am featuring the band in this series, despite the fact that they only released three studio albums. Even though there aren't many songs to choose from, this music is too important to be ignored. If just one person checks out the music and becomes a new fan, the effort will have been worth it.<br />
<br />
So why is Television's music so fantastic?<br />
<br />
<i>Marquee Moon</i> is the album I was referring to earlier. If you play it from start to finish, you'll hear things that are nothing like you have ever heard before. The title track is the highlight and has claims to being the best song ever made; well, the best song not by Modest Mouse at the very least. For the better part of 11 minutes, Tom Verlaine and Richard Lloyd weave together their intricate guitar parts until they become one. The song is both cool and magnificent, soaring to an unbelievable climax. The rest of the album isn't far behind.<br />
<br />
Television's guitar sound is unique. They unfairly get labelled as punk when this sound is so much more. That's probably because they were part of the CBGB scene with The Ramones, Talking Heads, Blondie and similar bands. I've only had the pleasure of seeing Television play on five or six occasions, including the most recent lineup with Jimmy Rip instead of Richard Lloyd. They are probably better now than when they were young men forty years ago. If you somehow get the chance to see them live, you have to take it.<br />
<br />
Billy Ficca is one hell of a drummer and Fred Smith's bass is a vital part of the overall sound. If you like alternative rock at all, you'll almost certainly love Television if you are not already a fan. For fans of traditional rock, there's a danger that Verlaine's reedy vocals will be too unusual for you to accept. But if you're a fan of David Byrne, you'll have no trouble.<br />
<br />
I would have to say that Television's <i>Live at the Old Waldorf</i> is the best live album ever released. It makes great songs even better. The 1978 show was released on CD in 2003, but was limited to 5,000 copies. There was also a Record Day vinyl release of 3,000 copies in 2011.<br />
<br />
<i>Adventure </i>was the follow up to <i>Marquee Moon</i>, but was not as well received. The only other studio album, <i>Television</i>, was released in 1992. It's almost unfair that these other albums were dismissed because they were compared to the masterful debut. The truth is that many of the songs from <i>Adventure </i>and <i>Television</i> come to life when played live.<br />
<br />
I hope you'll give these 20 songs a chance if you have never heard them. As usual the playlist can be found on my <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uCMyFkAPBuc&amp;list=PLBPVVpmmaxHl7EZ6G1YkpDPJ_faHhtUiA">YouTube channel</a>. Here is my Ultimate Playlist for Television:<br />
<br />
Ain't That Nothin'<br />Venus<br />Call Mr. Lee<br />Friction<br />The Fire<br />Little Johnny Jewel<br />No Glamour for Willi<br />Elevation<br />Adventure<br />Knockin&#8217; on Heaven&#8217;s Door<br />Shane, She Wrote This<br />The Dream's Dream<br />Torn Curtain<br />Glory<br />Guiding Light<br />1880 or So<br />See No Evil<br />Foxhole <br />Prove It <br />Marquee Moon<br />

</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-2966049132907887890' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-IZtfxDCxSY8/Vui9gVtshSI/AAAAAAAAFOc/zTBE5LMB15gXxehd45hMW_QZbcs9_p2wg/s1600/Jicks.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="424" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-IZtfxDCxSY8/Vui9gVtshSI/AAAAAAAAFOc/zTBE5LMB15gXxehd45hMW_QZbcs9_p2wg/s640/Jicks.jpg" width="640" /></a></div>
<br />
Have you ever been to a concert and wished the band had played certain 
songs? I know I have. The reality is, you'll never get exactly what you 
want. However, you are free to build any playlist you like. I have 
decided to write a series showcasing the Top 20 songs from some of my 
favorite bands.<br />
<br />
For those of you who are old enough to remember cassette tapes, you'll 
know the terrible anguish of trying to fit the songs together. How 
annoying when the last song you picked was still playing as the tape ran
 out! Those days are over and it's now a simple task to throw together a
 playlist using your computer, iPod or a USB. I know that having USB 
capability has enhanced my driving pleasure because it's so easy to 
update a list of songs.<br />
<br />
But there's also more to creating a playlist than simply deciding which 
songs to include. Like an actual concert, or even a single song, a good 
playlist features changes in mood and tempo. If a band opens with the 
three songs that the audience most wants to hear, the rest of the 
performance might fall flat. My own particular method of creating a 
playlist has a number of considerations:
<br />
<br />
<ul>
<li>Mix up the duration of the songs</li>
<li>Put space between songs from the same album, unless the two are better when played in sequence</li>
<li>Build to a natural high, slow it down again, and finish with a real flourish</li>
<li>Put in some newer songs close to the start, assuming there are newer songs worthy of inclusion</li>
<li>For bands with more than one vocalist, mix up the sequence depending on who is singing</li>
<li>Speed and style matters, so mix it up unless there is a good reason not to</li>
<li>Albums often have a great choice of opening and closing song that work best in that particular spot&nbsp;</li>
<li>The final three or four songs might resemble an encore if it was a real concert</li>
<li>Leave the listener satisfied and wanting more&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<br />
The first post-Pavement Stephen Malkmus album grabbed my attention from the start, but I lost my way for a while after that. <i>Pig Lib</i> and <i>Face the Truth</i> seemed decent enough, but I set them aside for years. Then came the release of <i>Real Emotional Trash</i> and everything changed. The band toured with Janet Weiss on drums and I found myself seeking out the earlier albums and enjoying the concerts more than ever before. <i>Mirror Traffic</i> and <i>Wig Out at Jagbags</i> contain plenty of great songs too, so I finally realized something that should have been obvious:<br />
<br />
Stephen Malkmus writes brilliant songs.<br />
<br />
The lyrics are full of humor. His quirky delivery is unmistakable and isn't dramatically different from his days with Pavement. Here's the shocking thing though; as much as I adore Pavement, I listen to the Jicks more often. There isn't a song that I want to skip. So on the rare days that I am not listening to Modest Mouse, it's the Jicks that are most likely to be my fall back option.<br />
<br />
Figuring out which songs to include in the final 20 for this playlist was far from easy. I found myself agonizing over which favorites to cut. Just listen to Malkmus play the guitar and you'll hear how distinctive his style is. The vocals may put off some, but I love his delivery. If you like alternative rock and guitar-driven songs, there's plenty to like here. If you're a fan of clever lyrics that make you smile, there's no shortage of those either.<br />
<br />

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://intheframefilmreviews.blogspot.com/2016/03/ultimate-playlists-5-joy-division.html'>Ultimate Playlists 5: Joy Division</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-5730673606111522848' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-2KjiX2R-xzg/VuNVu2r473I/AAAAAAAAFOM/GGNATeHAzAskdlUvX4NB_TvF8z3HuoHIQ/s1600/Joy%2BDivision.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="360" src="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-2KjiX2R-xzg/VuNVu2r473I/AAAAAAAAFOM/GGNATeHAzAskdlUvX4NB_TvF8z3HuoHIQ/s640/Joy%2BDivision.jpg" width="640" /></a></div>

songs? I know I have. The reality is, you'll never get exactly what you 
want. However, you are free to build any playlist you like. I have 
decided to write a series showcasing the Top 20 songs from some of my 
favorite bands.<br />

 playlist using your computer, iPod or a USB. I know that having USB

update a list of songs.<br />

songs to include. Like an actual concert, or even a single song, a good

playlist has a number of considerations:<br />
<br />
<br />
<ul>
<li>Mix up the duration of the songs</li>
<li>Put space between songs from the same album, unless the two are better when played in sequence</li>
<li>Build to a natural high, slow it down again, and finish with a real flourish</li>
<li>Put in some newer songs close to the start, assuming there are newer songs worthy of inclusion</li>

<li>The final three or four songs might resemble an encore if it was a real concert</li>
<li>Leave the listener satisfied and wanting more&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<br />
Joy Division formed in 1976 as Warsaw, releasing an album of the same name. I considered all of those songs for the purposes of this playlist. The band's sound started as something that wall slot neatly into the punk category, but evolved into a dark and majestic thing that I had never heard before. Interpol did a pretty good job of revisiting that style on their early albums, but I believe Joy Division to be in a league of their own.<br />
<br />
Ian Curtis was the lead singer and most of the songs deal with what he was feeling when he was in love with two different women. Other songs touch on such joyous subjects as epilepsy. I know a lot of people who think that Joy Division's music would be a good choice if you were planning on slitting your wrists, but I can't agree. This music just soars, despite the rough edges of Curtis' vocals. Peter Hook's bass is an essential element of the music and some songs feature bass leads. Bernard Sumner (guitar/keyboards) and Stephen Morris (drums) complete the lineup.<br />
<br />
For a band named after a Nazi brothel, with a lead singer who committed suicide, I find Joy Division's music surprisingly uplifting. The sounds is so dense and layered. If you check out the excellent movie, <a href="http://intheframefilmreviews.blogspot.ca/2011/05/control-fitting-tribute-to-ian-curtis.html">Control</a>, you'll almost certainly appreciate the music even more. These songs are about real things that mattered enormously to Curtis.<br />
<br />
With just two studio albums (plus <i>Warsaw</i>), compilations and live releases, we were robbed of what was at the time, a unique sound. As with all great artists, I find myself wondering what they would have produced had they been around much longer.<br />
<br />
I would urge any fan to go and hear Peter Hook and the Light perform these incredible songs. It's as close as you're going to get to the original sound if you weren't around at the time. I was at an <i>Unknown Pleasures</i> show a few years ago and every hair was standing on end. I'm hoping that Peter Hook will play <i>Closer </i>in Toronto before I die, but I'll be there this November for <i>Substance</i>.<br />
<br />
So let's get to the playlist. The order wasn't obvious for this one because with just two studio albums, you'll expect to hear the songs in the order from the album. I've created this with a live show in mind, with the last four tracks being the encore. If you're a fan, I would love to know what you think. Here's my <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2iKQ0MaRiOw&amp;list=PLBPVVpmmaxHklgCaMvqk7jIptVrqG1EJ9">YouTube playlist</a> of these 20 songs.<br />

</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-1176090711312846097' itemprop='description articleBody'>

three songs that the audience most wants to hear, the rest of the 
performance might fall flat. My own particular method of creating a 
playlist has a number of considerations:<br />
<br />
<ul>
<li>Mix up the duration of the songs</li>
<li>Put space between songs from the same album, unless the two are better when played in sequence</li>
<li>Build to a natural high, slow it down again, and finish with a real flourish</li>
<li>Put in some newer songs close to the start, assuming there are newer songs worthy of inclusion</li>
<li>For bands with more than one vocalist, mix up the sequence depending on who is singing</li>
<li>Speed and style matters, so mix it up unless there is a good reason not to</li>
<li>Albums often have a great choice of opening and closing song that work best in that particular spot&nbsp;</li>
<li>The final three or four songs might resemble an encore if it was a real concert</li>
<li>Leave the listener satisfied and wanting more&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<br />
Pixies formed in 1986 and released four full-length albums, an EP and several singles before breaking up in 1993. In 2004, the band reformed with its original lineup, allowing a new generation of fans to hear the music live. Kurt Cobain once remarked that Nirvana were just ripping off the basic Pixies style.<br />
<br />
I remember the band announcing a 2004 UK tour and tickets selling out in 8 minutes. Luckily, I secured mine. 2013 saw a new album, but bassist Kim Deal has since left the band.<br />
<br />
Not many bands hook me instantly the first time I hear their music, but Pixies did that to me when I first heard <i>Vamos</i>. I bought everything they had ever released within a few days of that initial exposure.<br />
<br />
Choosing 20 songs for this playlist was almost impossible. Even being a strict as I possibly could, I was still struggling to cut the final 11 songs until this morning. In truth, a Pixies playlist would probably contain more songs than just 20, but I'll stick to that format for this series for the sake of consistency.<br />
<br />
The band's sound ranges from rant, to pop, to rock, but there is something else, almost beyond description in some of the songs. There is an almost mystical quality that suggests searching for something just beyond reach. I might describe it as atmospheric, but that doesn't do it justice. Many of these songs make my hair stand up on end. The basic structure is so simple, but that doesn't make it any less essential.<br />

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://intheframefilmreviews.blogspot.com/2016/03/ultimate-playlists-3-pavement.html'>Ultimate Playlists 3: Pavement</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-4176355571939744614' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-sAFLwX9XUsw/VtxkCPEadaI/AAAAAAAAFNk/pNNOFCzQUi0/s1600/pavement.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="426" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-sAFLwX9XUsw/VtxkCPEadaI/AAAAAAAAFNk/pNNOFCzQUi0/s640/pavement.jpg" width="640" /></a></div>
<br />
Have you ever been to a concert and wished the band had played certain 
songs? I know I have. The reality is, you'll never get exactly what you 
want. However, you are free to build any playlist you like. I have 
decided to write a series showcasing the Top 20 songs from some of my 
favorite bands.<br />
<br />
For those of you who are old enough to remember cassette tapes, you'll 
know the terrible anguish of trying to fit the songs together. How 
annoying when the last song you picked was still playing as the tape ran
 out! Those days are over and it's now a simple task to throw together a
 playlist using your computer, iPod or a USB. I know that having USB 
capability has enhanced my driving pleasure because it's so easy to 
update a list of songs.<br />
<br />
But there's also more to creating a playlist than simply deciding which 
songs to include. Like an actual concert, or even a single song, a good 
playlist features changes in mood and tempo. If a band opens with the 
three songs that the audience most wants to hear, the rest of the 
performance might fall flat. My own particular method of creating a 
playlist has a number of considerations:<br />
<br />
<br />
<ul>
<li>Mix up the duration of the songs</li>
<li>Put space between songs from the same album, unless the two are better when played in sequence</li>
<li>Build to a natural high, slow it down again, and finish with a real flourish</li>
<li>Put in some newer songs close to the start, assuming there are newer songs worthy of inclusion</li>
<li>For bands with more than one vocalist, mix up the sequence depending on who is singing</li>
<li>Speed and style matters, so mix it up unless there is a good reason not to</li>
<li>Albums often have a great choice of opening and closing song that work best in that particular spot&nbsp;</li>
<li>The final three or four songs might resemble an encore if it was a real concert</li>
<li>Leave the listener satisfied and wanting more&nbsp;</li>
</ul>
<br />
Pavement formed in 1989, but didn't release their first full-length album until 1992. I wasn't aware of the band until the British music press and a friend informed me that they were ripping off my favorite band at the time, The Fall. While that's certainly true to some extent, Pavement's sound evolved into something much more. I now consider them one of my Top 5 bands and they are definitely higher on that list than The Fall.<br />
<br />
Although they technically used at least three vocalists during live shows, Stephen Malkmus was easily the biggest influence on their sound. His unusual guitar style almost resembles speech in some songs and he's continued to churn out fantastic songs for more than 20 years. But let's focus on Pavement for the purposes of this installment.<br />
<br />
The band's first three full albums contain the vast majority of their best songs. You can make an argument for <i>Slanted and Enchanted</i> or <i>Crooked Rain, Crooked Rain</i> being the best of those, but my personal favorite is <i>Wowee Zowee</i>. I'm a weird guy with unconventional tastes and it doesn't surprise me in the least that I love listening to Malkmus sing. He changes key almost randomly, but it somehow works; just listen to <i>Here </i>and you'll hear how raw his vocals can be. <br />
<br />
<i>Wowee Zowee</i> was a departure from the sound on the first two albums. It's longer and employs a variety of styles. The fourth word in the opening song is 'castration' so I don't think it was supposed to be a feel-good album. That said, some of the tracks put the listener in chill-out mode, while others range from prog rock to outright rant or country. My favorite Pavement track, <i>Rattled By the Rush</i> is the second track on the album.<br />
<br />
