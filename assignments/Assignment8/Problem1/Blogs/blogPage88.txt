<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://theidealcopy.blogspot.com/2016/03/end-of-era-ffxi-servers-shut-down-for.html'>End of an Era: FFXI servers shut down for consoles: Xbox 360, PlayStation 2</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-4750178903937497398' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<span class="remaining-body">(from the <b>Adventures in Vana'diel</b> department)<br /><br />So - it finally had to happen.  SquareEnix shut down the remaining servers for the console versions of Final Fantasy XI this morning, leaving it PC-only for now.<br /><br /> Full info here: <a class="linkified" href="http://www.playonline.com/ff11us/envi/win/winshift.html" rel="nofollow noreferrer" target="_blank">http://www.playonline.com/ff11us/envi/win/winshift.html</a><br /><br /> I remember getting FFXI for my PlayStation 2 - it came with the Sony PlayStation 2 HDD and the promise of a whole new world of PlayStation features.  Unfortunately, right after launch, Sony canceled their US plans, and the Japanese features like storing games on the hard drive, and the Sony Broadband Navigator never came to these shores.  Still - it was a rock solid FFXI platform, and I spent many hours exploring the world of Vana'diel on my PS2.<br /><br /> When the Xbox 360 client came out, it was a blast to have an MMORPG on that platform.   There were some odd issues with Xbox Live integration, and a couple of performance quirks, but the true widescreen and increased draw distance made it the FFXI platform of choice until the PC hardware and software options finally overtook it.<br /><br /> I played both console versions last night, archiving all of my screen shots and other ephemera.  It was a bittersweet farewell.  The servers were definitely overloaded - but it was still a lot of fun to run around Vana'diel and look at the sights one last time.<br /><br /> FFXI is going to continue on PC for the near future.  Also - SquareEnix has announced plans for a full mobile version in the next year or so.  This means adventurers will continue to fight the good fight, and explore the world of Vana'diel - but it's definitely the end of the console era for FFXI.</span> <br />
<br />
See you in Windurst!<br />
<br />
Cheers,<br />
Paul<br />

(This is a quick post - these instructions are necessarily terse and mainly serve as a reminder to myself. If you do try this - you do so at your own risk - I can't provide any help if you mess anything up.) <br />
<br />
I needed to set up Oracle Java JRE 8 on my Ubuntu 12.04 LTS 64-bit system.<br />
<br />
For previous versions of Oracle Java, I followed the steps in "6. Installation from RPM package" on the web page here:<br />
<a href="http://how-to.linuxcareer.com/oracle-java-jdk-7-on-ubuntu-linux-installation" target="_blank">http://how-to.linuxcareer.com/oracle-java-jdk-7-on-ubuntu-linux-installation</a><br />
<br />
These instructions worked fine for previous versions of Java but broke with JRE 8.<br />
<br />
When I analyzed the failure, I saw it was breaking with the alternatives command.&nbsp; Ubuntu uses update-alternatives instead of alternatives.<br />
<br />
So - I decided to try something bold.&nbsp; I created a symbolic link to alternatives from update-alternatives:<br />
<br />
<span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">cd /usr/sbin</span><br />
<span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">sudo ln -s update-alternatives alternatives</span><br />
<br />
Then, I followed the steps in "6. Installation from RPM package".&nbsp; The package installed properly.&nbsp; Just for reference, here's the commands I used for the JRE 8u77 I installed today:<br />
<br />
<span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">sudo alien --scripts jre-8u77-linux-x64.rpm</span><br />
<span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">sudo dpkg -i jre1.8.0-77_1.8.077-1_amd64.deb</span><br />
<br />
That left connecting up the Firefox Java plugin.&nbsp; There's many ways to do that, but I like using alternatives.. er... I mean update-alternatives:<br />
<br />
<span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">sudo update-alternatives --install /usr/lib/mozilla/plugins/libjavaplugin.so libjavaplugin.so /usr/java/default/lib/amd64/libnpjp2.so 20</span><br />
<br />
Obviously - these are for a 64 bit system.&nbsp; These won't work as written for a 32 bit system or a mixed 64/32 bit system.<br />
<br />
Good luck! <br />
<br />
Cheers,<br />
Paul&nbsp; <br />

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://theidealcopy.blogspot.com/2016/03/gamestop-good-news-for-retro-gamers.html'>GameStop: Good News For Retro Gamers</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-5139658726721859158' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<b><u>tl;dr</u>: GameStop online is now selling retro/vintage games and accessories</b><br />
<br />
When I was in GameStop this weekend, I noticed a flyer at the desk offering trade-in deals for NES, Genesis, SNES, Dreamcasts and PlayStations.  I asked the clerk about it, and he said that they're using this to get retro gear for their on-line site.  I found the link here:<br />
<br />
<a class="linkified" href="http://www.gamestop.com/collection/retro-classics" rel="nofollow noreferrer" target="_blank">http://www.gamestop.com/collection/retro-classics</a><br />
<br />
This is a change in their policy where they would discontinuing selling older systems when newer systems were released.<br />
<div>
<br /></div>
<div>
<u>UPDATE</u>: &nbsp;So - I ordered a Dreamcast game - D2 - from them.  It arrived promptly, and the shipping container was a sturdy cardboard box with air pillows.  They do warn you that the game packaging may not be perfect.  In this case, it contained the original jewel box - definitely used, but not trashed.  The booklet was included, as was the tray art.  The hub for the fourth disk was broken, so they put the fourth disk in a separate envelope.  The discs themselves appeared to be in good condition.<br />
<br />
All in all - pleased with this service.  We will have to see how the prices compare over time.</div>
<div>
<br /></div>
<div>
Cheers,</div>
<div>
Paul<br />

<h2 class='date-header'><span>Thursday, March 10, 2016</span></h2>

          <div class="date-posts">
        
<div class='post-outer'>
<div class='post hentry uncustomized-post-template' itemprop='blogPost' itemscope='itemscope' itemtype='http://schema.org/BlogPosting'>

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://theidealcopy.blogspot.com/2016/04/quick-review-rise-of-tomb-raider.html'>Quick Review: Rise of the Tomb Raider</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-3975433472767239145' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<b><u>tl;dr</u>: If you have an Xbox One - or in a surprise port, an Xbox 360 - and you're a fan of the new gritty Tomb Raider - or survival action in general - consider picking this up.</b><br />
<br />
The storyline isn't quite as compelling, and if you play it straight through, you might find it a little shorter, but it takes the open world elements of Tomb Raider and enlarges them.  There are many little side quests and things to do in this game world.  Plus - some actual tombs to raid this time.  <br />
<br />
It's rated mature, and although it's not quite as violent as Tomb Raider, Lara still does a lot of killing in this one. <br />
<br />
They just released a Windows version as well - available via Steam or the Windows 10 store.&nbsp; <br />
<br />
<u>Warning</u>:  if your rig is slightly older, this will push it pretty hard, due to the huge open world spaces and all their fine detail.  Unlike Tomb Raider, which runs on my rig set to ultra, I had to turn down some of the graphic settings for Rise Of The Tomb Raider.  There are basic presets you can use which you can optimize for your hardware.  <br />
<br />
The previous paragraph really makes me appreciate my consoles even more. <br />
<br />
I did play (and finish) this on the 360, and although the graphics are definitely dialed down, the frame rate is rock solid and it's eminently playable.  If you're still rocking the 360, you won't be disappointed by this one, even though it doesn't quite look as snazzy as its predecessor, due to the open world demands.<br />
<br />
<b> I rate it a solid headshot with a compound bow. </b><br />
<br />
More reviews here:<br />
<a class="linkified" href="http://www.metacritic.com/game/pc/rise-of-the-tomb-raider" rel="nofollow noreferrer" target="_blank">http://www.metacritic.com/game/pc/rise-of-the-tomb-raider</a><br />
<br />

<h2 class='date-header'><span>Monday, February 15, 2016</span></h2>

          <div class="date-posts">
        
<div class='post-outer'>
<div class='post hentry uncustomized-post-template' itemprop='blogPost' itemscope='itemscope' itemtype='http://schema.org/BlogPosting'>
<meta content='7375045983637093649' itemprop='blogId'/>
<meta content='5618320281235321327' itemprop='postId'/>

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://theidealcopy.blogspot.com/2016/02/turn-your-ubuntu-1204-lts-into-steamos.html'>Turn your Ubuntu 12.04 LTS into SteamOS (kinda)</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-5618320281235321327' itemprop='description articleBody'>
(from the <b>This is a cool trick</b> department)<br />
<br />
When I first heard about Steam for Linux, I immediately looked at their website and decided to try it.&nbsp; Here's the specifications from the original page: <a href="https://github.com/ValveSoftware/steam-for-linux" target="_blank">https://github.com/ValveSoftware/steam-for-linux</a><br />
<br />
Steam for Linux requires the following:<br />
<ul>
<li>1 GHz Pentium 4 or AMD Opteron with 512 megabytes of RAM and 5 gigabytes of hard drive space, or better</li>
<li>Internet connection (Cable/DSL speeds recommended)</li>
<li>Ubuntu 12.04 LTS, fully updated</li>
<li>Latest graphics driver</li>
<li>NVidia driver support - For recent cards (e.g. series 8), you will 
need to install 310.x. For older cards, driver 304.x supports the NVidia
 6 and 7 GPU series. To access these drivers, first update 
your&nbsp;cache&nbsp;and then install the specific&nbsp;<a href="https://support.steampowered.com/kb_article.php?ref=8509-RFXM-1964" target="_blank">driver</a>&nbsp;you need from the list in Additional Drivers.</li>
<li>AMD driver support - For recent cards (e.g. series 5 and above), we 
recommend installing the 12.11 driver. For older cards, Catalyst 13.1 
Legacy supports the HD 2400 Pro card and is the latest for the 2 and 4 
GPU series.</li>
<li>Intel HD 3000/4000 driver support - you will need to use the latest 
Mesa drivers, Mesa 9 or later. For installation instructions, see&nbsp;<a href="http://wiki.ubuntu.com/Valve" target="_blank">here</a>.</li>
</ul>
So -&nbsp; I added a new hard disc to my Windows gaming system, and created a stand-alone Ubuntu 12.04 LTS installation.<br />
<br />
Then - I heard about SteamOS.&nbsp; It's built on Debian - like Ubuntu.&nbsp; I was curious what it was like, but - hey - I'd already fine tuned my Ubuntu system - and I didn't feel like re-inventing the wheel.<br />
<br />
That's where it stayed - until tonight.<br />
<br />
I saw an article or two about Marc Deslauriers and a project he'd been working on: <a href="https://plus.google.com/103632865447092840471/posts/1nVUjx8WtWC" target="_blank">https://plus.google.com/103632865447092840471/posts/1nVUjx8WtWC</a><br />
<br />
"I've rebuilt a couple of <a class="ot-hashtag aaTEdf" href="https://plus.google.com/s/%23SteamOS" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">#SteamOS</a>
 packages in a PPA for Ubuntu 12.04 LTS. They add a "SteamOS" session 
that runs Valve's compositor and starts Steam in big picture mode."<br />
<br />
There was an explanation of what this means here:&nbsp; <a href="http://www.webupd8.org/2013/12/install-steamos-session-in-ubuntu.html" target="_blank">http://www.webupd8.org/2013/12/install-steamos-session-in-ubuntu.html</a><br />
<br />
This had instructions on how to install it:<br />
<a href="http://askubuntu.com/questions/302488/can-i-run-steam-as-its-own-standalone-session" target="_blank">http://askubuntu.com/questions/302488/can-i-run-steam-as-its-own-standalone-session</a><br />
<br />
but they only work for Ubuntu 14.04, so here's the 12.04 instructions.&nbsp; <br />
<br />
<b>NOTE:&nbsp; Try this at your own risk.&nbsp; It worked for me - but I can't guarantee it will work for you. Nor can I provide any tech support if your system is rendered unusable.</b><br />
<br />
Enter these three commands in a terminal window - each sudo command should be one line - don't add any extra line breaks:<br />
<br />
<span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">sudo add-apt-repository ppa:mdeslaur/steamos</span><br />
<span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">sudo apt-get update</span><br />
<span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">sudo apt-get install steamos-compositor steamos-modeswitch-inhibitor steamos-xpad-dkms</span><br />
<br />
This will add a SteamOS login to the Ubuntu login screen.&nbsp; Selecting it will launch Steam in Big Picture mode without the overhead of a normal Linux window manager.<br />
<br />
I haven't fully tested this yet.&nbsp; It seems to be behaving itself - I'll update this post as I find out more.&nbsp; And - you can always remove it by<br />
<br />
<span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">sudo apt-get remove steamos-compositor steamos-modeswitch-inhibitor steamos-xpad-dkms</span><br />
<br />
and your system will be back to normal, pending a reboot.<br />
<br />
<b>UPDATE:&nbsp; </b>After running in SteamOS mode, then rebooting and starting Unity, my Ubuntu 12.04 LTS system lost the use of the Volume Up, Volume Down and Volume Mute keys on my multimedia keyboard.&nbsp; This was a real head scratcher - none of the online guides seemed to work - and some of the fixes were downright dangerous.&nbsp;<br />
<br />
I finally got my keys back by following the advice here: <a href="http://askubuntu.com/questions/387379/ubuntu-12-04-volume-up-down-mute-stopped-working" target="_blank">http://askubuntu.com/questions/387379/ubuntu-12-04-volume-up-down-mute-stopped-working</a> and deleting my ~/.pulse directory.&nbsp; You'll want to reboot immediately after doing this. &nbsp;<br />
<br />
Apparently, running in SteamOS mode disables the volume keys.&nbsp; Deleting the ~/.pulse folder and rebooting recovers it safely.&nbsp; You'll just have to reset your audio output next time you log into Unity/Gnome.<br />
<br />
Cheers,<br />
Paul<br />
