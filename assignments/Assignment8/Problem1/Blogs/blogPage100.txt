<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://ws-dl.blogspot.com/2016/04/2016-04-05-cni-spring-2016-trip-report.html'>2016-04-05: CNI Spring 2016 Trip Report</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-7114153616715816132' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-UDvcMAmmkYI/VwQsDpQpE2I/AAAAAAAABzg/glFWOxOHl6g5hoP79c2QUYGe4BIhAVMSw/s1600/cni-logo.gif" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-UDvcMAmmkYI/VwQsDpQpE2I/AAAAAAAABzg/glFWOxOHl6g5hoP79c2QUYGe4BIhAVMSw/s1600/cni-logo.gif" /></a></div>
The <a href="https://www.cni.org/events/membership-meetings/upcoming-meeting/spring-2016">CNI Spring 2016 Members Meeting</a> was held in San Antonio, TX, April 4-5, 2016.&nbsp; As usual, the presentations were excellent but with six or more simultaneous sessions you are forced to make hard choices about what to catch up on.<br />
<br />
This year <a href="https://dean.library.unt.edu/Dean:About">Martin Halbert</a> and <a href="https://educopia.org/staff/katherine-skinner">Katherine Skinner</a> arranged the "<a href="http://freegovinfo.info/node/10979">Digital Preservation of Federal Information Summit</a>", convening 30+ people to discuss "...the topic of preservation and access to at-risk digital government information."&nbsp; It was quite the collaborative exercise, and I know Martin produced some summary slides that I will link here when they are posted.&nbsp; There were only a few presentations (and they were done in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PechaKucha">Pecha Kucha</a> format) for this Summit, and I was fortunate enough to give one for Herbert and I entitled "Why We Need Multiple Archives".&nbsp; The answer is probably pretty obvious for the crowd that Martin assembled, but we often run into people that don't understand the role of archives beyond that of the (obviously excellent) Internet Archive.<br />
<br />
<iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="485" marginheight="0" marginwidth="0" scrolling="no" src="//www.slideshare.net/slideshow/embed_code/key/3Z0cD6bsLWHrQm" style="border-width: 1px; border: 1px solid #CCC; margin-bottom: 5px; max-width: 100%;" width="595"> </iframe> <br />
<div style="margin-bottom: 5px;">
<b> <a href="https://www.slideshare.net/phonedude/why-we-need-multiple-archives" target="_blank" title="Why We Need Multiple Archives">Why We Need Multiple Archives</a> </b> from <b><a href="https://www.slideshare.net/phonedude" target="_blank">Michael Nelson</a></b> </div>
<br />
<br />
<a href="https://twitter.com/victoriastodden">Victoria Stodden</a> gave the opening keynote, "<a href="http://web.stanford.edu/~vcs/talks/CNI-April4-2016-STODDEN.pdf">Defining the Scholarly Record for Computational Research</a>", in which she talked about the "<a href="https://web.stanford.edu/~vcs/papers/LFRSR12012008.pdf">Reproducible Research Standard</a>", <a href="http://resarchcompendia.org/">ResarchCompendia.org</a>, and computational infrastructure within the context of legal and social norms.&nbsp; CNI will eventually put the videos up, in the mean time I would encourage you to see her <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UyNYP8tMRvI">SC15 talk</a> that touches on similar themes.<br />
<br />
The next session I attended was <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/jasonvarghese">Jason Varghese</a> (NYPL) presenting "<a href="https://www.cni.org/topics/information-access-retrieval/microservices-architecture-building-scalable-library-software-solutions">Microservices Architecture: Building Scalable (Library) Software Solutions</a>." He's clearly doing cool stuff, but I would have appreciated a more detailed discussion of the APIs they've implemented, but I guess that can be found at: <a href="http://api.repo.nypl.org/">http://api.repo.nypl.org/</a>. <br />
<br />
The next session was "<a href="https://www.cni.org/topics/learning-spaces/scaling-maker-spaces-across-the-web-weaving-maker-space-communities-together-to-support-distributed-networked-collaboration-in-knowledge-creation">Scaling Maker Spaces Across the Web: Weaving Maker Space Communities Together to Support Distributed, Networked Collaboration in Knowledge Creation</a>", by <a href="https://vpr-norman.ou.edu/users/rick-luce">Rick Luce</a> and <a href="http://thoughts.care-affiliates.com/">Carl Grant</a>, both at Oklahoma University.&nbsp; They talked about their experiences setting up a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hackerspace">makerspace</a> (complete with 3D printing and VR capabilities) in the library, both a small satellite for their on campus library (the "<a href="https://libraries.ou.edu/edge">edge</a>") and their much larger facility in the research park two miles away (the "<a href="http://www.ou.edu/innovationhub/">hub</a>").&nbsp; I urge you to peruse the links -- this was truly impressive stuff &amp; Rick consistently does exciting things with libraries.<br />
<br />
I skipped the final session of the day in order to get my slides for Tuesday morning arranged.&nbsp; I had originally thought I had a 30 minute slot, but in reality I had 15 minutes and many slides needed tossing.&nbsp; There was an evening reception and they we had dinner at one of the many restaurants on the famed <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Antonio_River_Walk">River Walk</a>.<br />
<br />
Tuesday began with split sessions, and I was in the session that Martin Halbert arranged, "<a href="https://www.cni.org/topics/digital-curation/national-web-archiving-programs-in-the-u-s">National Web Archiving Programs in the U.S.</a>", along with <a href="https://twitter.com/jefferson_bail">Jefferson Bailey</a> and <a href="http://Mark Phillips">Mark Phillips</a>.&nbsp; Jefferson gave a brief overview of the "<a href="https://blog.archive.org/2015/10/08/imls-national-digital-platform-grant-awarded-to-advance-web-archiving/">Systems Interoperability and Collaborative Development for Web Archiving</a>" project, Mark reviewed <a href="http://uc3-eotarchive-stg.cdlib.org/">End of Term (EOT) web archiving</a>, and Martin recapped the "Digital Preservation of Federal Information Summit" from the previous days.&nbsp; I presented a brief status about our work using Storytelling interfaces for summarizing collections in Archive-It:<br />
<br />

<div style="margin-bottom: 5px;">
<b> <a href="https://www.slideshare.net/phonedude/storytelling-for-summarizing-collections-in-web-archives" target="_blank" title="Storytelling for Summarizing Collections in Web Archives">Storytelling for Summarizing Collections in Web Archives</a> </b> from <b><a href="https://www.slideshare.net/phonedude" target="_blank">Michael Nelson</a></b> </div>
<br />
<br />
Unfortunately, with the simultaneous sessions I had to miss "<a href="http://www.slideshare.net/hvdsomp/dbpedia-archive-using-memento-triple-pattern-fragments-and-hdt">DBpedia Archive using Memento, Triple Pattern Fragments, and HDT</a>", presented by <a href="https://twitter.com/hvdsomp">Herbert Van de Sompel</a> and <a href="https://twitter.com/Miel_vds">Miel Vander Sande</a>.<br />
<br />
The next session I attended was about organization identifiers, and featured <a href="https://twitter.com/gbilder">Geoffery Bilder</a> (<a href="http://crossref.org/">Crossref</a>), <a href="https://www.datacite.org/news/datacite-appoints-patricia-cruse-executive-director.html">Patricia Cruse</a> (<a href="https://www.datacite.org/">DataCite</a>), and (via facetime) <a href="https://twitter.com/haakyak">Laure Haak</a> (<a href="http://orcid.org/">ORCID</a>).&nbsp; They are in the early stages of collaborating for org ids, and while I learned a lot, I would have appreciated a more thorough review of existing org id efforts and how they fall short of their goals.&nbsp; Part They did share their <a href="https://t.co/7lJwMucLxq">requirements document</a> and invited contributions.&nbsp; "Challenges Presented by Organizational IDs" by <a href="http://www.oclc.org/research/people/smith-yoshimura.html">Karen Smith-Yoshimura</a> (OCLC), from <a href="https://www.cni.org/events/membership-meetings/past-meetings/spring-2015">CNI Spring 2015</a>, provides some of the background that I did not have.<br />
<br />
The after lunch session that I attended on Tuesday was "<a href="https://www.cni.org/topics/digital-humanities/rebuilding-the-getty-provenance-index-as-linked-data">Rebuilding the Getty Provenance Index as Linked Data</a>".&nbsp; I knew almost nothing about the art world going into this, so now I know more about the linked data challenges of porting Getty's legacy databases that await <a href="https://twitter.com/azaroth42">Rob Sanderson</a> when he <a href="https://twitter.com/azaroth42/status/717102463237496832">joins Getty</a> later this month.<br />
<br />
The closing keynote, "<a href="https://www.cni.org/events/membership-meetings/upcoming-meeting/spring-2016/plenary-sessions-s16#closing">Activist Stewardship: The Imperative of Risk in Collecting Cultural Heritage</a>", was handled by a trio from UCLA: <a href="https://twitter.com/liber8er">Todd Grappone</a>, <a href="https://twitter.com/emcaulay">Elizabeth McAulay</a>, <a href="https://twitter.com/hbtravels">Heather Bristo</a>n (who was pinch hitting for <a href="https://twitter.com/FarbThink">Sharon Farb</a>).&nbsp; They presented about the <a href="http://digital.library.ucla.edu/dep/">Digital Ephemera Project</a>, and in general the role of archivists in collecting materials that will get you (the library) or the contributor in trouble.&nbsp; Some examples included the internal and external pressures about <a href="http://www.oac.cdlib.org/findaid/ark:/13030/kt4580212s/entire_text/">UCLA's Scientology collection</a> and contributors regarding the <a href="http://digital.library.ucla.edu/dep/green-movement/">Green Movement</a> collection.&nbsp; Cliff Lynch gave a good introduction to this session and promised a wrap for it as well, but the session ran a bit long and that did not happen.&nbsp; Rather than try to further summarize, I'll link the video when it comes out.&nbsp; I did appreciate that Memento got a mention in the presentation regarding finding archived images embedded in tweets that had otherwise been deleted!<br />

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://ws-dl.blogspot.com/2016/03/2016-03-22-language-detection-where-to.html'>2016-03-22: Language Detection: Where to start?</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-6553124712582938511' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<html>

<style>

p.solid {border-style: solid;font-family:courier;font-size:80%;padding: 5px;}

</style>

<body>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a href="http://ux.prattsils.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/10/language-header.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-c9esVZhXggs/VuYO4Lmqc-I/AAAAAAAAALg/7mJ3iX7A5hECpzOZqUNWfaQW3Hz0YgKSg/s320/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-03-13%2Bat%2B9.07.24%2BPM.png" /></a></div>

<p>
Language detection is not a simple task, and no method results in 100% accuracy. You can find different packages online to detect different languages. I have used some methods and tools to detect the language of either websites or some texts. Here is a review of methods I came across during working on my JCDL 2015 paper, <a href="http://www.cs.odu.edu/~mln/pubs/jcdl-2015/jcdl-2015-arabic-sites.pdf">How Well are Arabic Websites Archived?</a>. Here I discuss detecting a webpage's language using the HTTP language header and the HTML language tag. In addition, I reviewed several language detection packages, including <a href="https://pypi.python.org/pypi/guess-language">Guess-Language</a>, <a href="http://github.com/decultured/Python-Language-Detector">Python-Language Detector</a>, <a href="https://github.com/saffsd/langid.py">LangID</a> and <a href="https://detectlanguage.com">Google Language Detection API</a>. And since Python is my favorite coding language I searched for tools that were written in Python.<br />
<br/>

I found that a primary way to detect the language of a webpage is to use the HTTP language header and the HTML language tag. However, only a small percentage of pages include the language tag and sometimes the detected language is affected by the browser setting.

<a href="https://pypi.python.org/pypi/guess-language">Guess-Language</a> and <a href="http://github.com/decultured/Python-Language-Detector">Python-Language Detector</a> are fast in detecting language, but they are more accurate with more text. Also, you have to extract the HTML tags before passing the text to the tools.

<a href="https://github.com/saffsd/langid.py">LangID</a> is a tool that detects language and gives you a confidence score, it's fast and works well with short texts and it is easy to install and use.

<a href="https://detectlanguage.com">Google Language Detection API</a> is also a powerful tool that can be downloaded for different programming languages, it also has a confidence score, but you need to sign in and if the dataset you need to detect is large, (larger than 5000 requests a day (1 MB/day)), you must choose a <a href="https://detectlanguage.com/plans">payable plan</a>.</br>

<br />

<span style="color: blue;">HTTP Language Header:</span><br />
If you want to detect the language of a web site a primary method is to look at the HTTP response header, <a href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc3282">Content-Language</a>. The Content-Language lets you know what languages are present on the requested page. The value is defined as a two or three letter <a href="https://www.loc.gov/standards/iso639-2/php/code_list.php">language code</a> (such as &#8216;fr&#8217; for French), and sometimes followed by a country code (such as &#8216;fr-CA&#8217; for French spoken in Canada).<br />

<br />For example:<br />

<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="http://www.bagergade-bogb.dk/" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-W8vJ328LlTo/VuYL-XQXqHI/AAAAAAAAALA/VaYRMHbq5KARM2lYkvjMSKhXAjcTOoBng/s320/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-03-13%2Bat%2B8.49.28%2BPM.png" /></a></div>

<p class="solid">
curl -I --silent http://bagergade-bogb.dk/ |grep -i "Content-Language"<br /><br/>

Content-Language: da-DK,da-DK<br />
</p>
In this example the webpage's language is Danish (Denmark).</br><br/>

In some cases you will find some sites offering content in multiple languages, and the Content-Language header only specifies one of the languages.</br>

<br />For example:<br />
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.hotelrenania.it/" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/--dBJ-AZA9BE/VuYOizI0MVI/AAAAAAAAALU/bCeqgDCbVjAI3RJMJt_aBzNGjUjfL6jRA/s320/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-03-13%2Bat%2B9.01.32%2BPM.png" /></a></div>

<p class="solid">
curl -I&nbsp; --silent http://www.hotelrenania.it/ |grep -i "Content-Language"<br /><br />

Content-Language: it<br />
</p>
In this example, when looking at the browser the webpage has three languages available Italian, English and Dutch. And it only states Italian as its Content-Language.</br></br>

You have to note that the Content-Language does not always match the language displayed in your bowser, because the browser's displayed language depends on the <a href="https://www.w3.org/International/questions/qa-lang-priorities">browser's language preference</a> which you can change. <br />

<br/>For example:<br />

<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a href="https://www.debian.org/" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-__wtJgIECC8/VuuuGVDgFII/AAAAAAAAAMg/JNlbp2yhC5I_cLIhVJ3lykFXWzlDWgBCg/s320/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-03-18%2Bat%2B3.18.25%2BAM.png" /></a></div>

<p class="solid">
curl -I --silent https://www.debian.org/ |grep -i "Content-Language"<br /><br/>

Content-Language: en<br />
</p>

This webpage offers its content in more than 37 different languages. Here I had my browsers language preference set as Arabic, and the Content-Language found was English. </br>

<br/>
In addition, most cases the Content-Language is not included in the header. From a random sample of 10,000 English websites in DMOZ I found that only 5.09% have the Content-Language header.
<br />

<br/>For example:<br />

<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.odu.edu" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-Gf9aNy8-krE/VuYPeFy6BXI/AAAAAAAAALo/Siu35RC1Gxc4pO3BqIL6CSzWt3UNToUOA/s320/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-03-13%2Bat%2B9.08.50%2BPM.png" /></a></div>

<p class="solid">
curl -I --silent http://www.odu.edu |grep -i "Content-Language"<br /><br />

<br />
</p> 
In this example we see that the Content-Language header was not found.<br/>

<br />
<span style="color: blue;">HTML Language:</span><br />
Another indication of the language of a web page is the HTML language tag (such as, &lt;html language='en'&gt;&#8230;.&lt;/html&gt;). Using this method will require you to save the HTML code first then search for the HTML language code.<br />

<br/>For example:<br />

<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a href="http://ksu.edu.sa/" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" src="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-wHgCCzx21bI/VuYP1MfZ3CI/AAAAAAAAALw/Az-RrTrKiPgzJBuIrid_c5IEaGxzsrHtQ/s320/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-03-13%2Bat%2B9.11.15%2BPM.png" /></a></div>

<p class="solid">
curl -I &#8212;silent http://ksu.edu.sa/ &gt; ksu.txt<br /><br />

grep "&lt;html lang=" ksu.txt<br /><br />

&lt;html lang="ar"" dir="rtl" class="no-js" &gt;<br />
</p>

However, I found from a random sample of 10,000 English websites in DMOZ directory that only 48.6% have the HTML language tag.<br />

<br />
<span style="color: blue;">Guess-Language:</span><br />
One tool to detect language in Python is <a href="https://pypi.python.org/pypi/guess-language">Guess-Language</a>. It detects the nature of the Unicode text. This tool <a href="https://code.google.com/p/guess-language/source/browse/trunk/guess_language/trigrams/">detects over 60 languages</a>. However, two important notes to be taken are that 1)this tool works better with more text and 2) don&#8217;t include the HTML tags in the text or the result will be flawed. So if you wanted to check the language of a webpage I recommend that you filter the tags using the <a href="http://www.crummy.com/software/BeautifulSoup/bs4/doc/">beautiful soup package</a> and then pass it to the tool.<br />

<br/>For example:<br />
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a href="http://abelian.org/tssp/" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-pxnxYjyrPtI/VuYQJQqUh3I/AAAAAAAAAL0/C7rufWbA_EYKsnaWVTNY4qtWBkiTyIwaQ/s320/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-03-13%2Bat%2B9.12.44%2BPM.png" /></a></div>

<p class="solid">
curl --silent http://abelian.org/tssp/|grep "title"|sed -e 's/&lt;[^&gt;]*&gt;//g'<br /><br />

Tesla Secondary Simulation Project<br />
</p>

<p class="solid">
python<br />
from guess_language import guessLanguage<br />
guessLanguage(&#8220;Tesla Secondary Simulation Project&#8221;)<br />
&#8217;fr&#8217;<br />
guessLanguage("Home Page An Internet collaboration exploring the physics of Tesla resonatorsThis project was started in May 2000 with the aim of exploring the fascinating physics of self-resonant single-layer solenoids as used for Tesla transformer secondaries.We maintain a precision software model of the solenoid which is based on our best theoretical understanding of the physics. This model provides a platform for theoretical work and virtual experiments.")<br /><br />

&#8217;en'<br />
</p>

This example shows detecting the title language of a randomly selected English webpage from DMOZ&nbsp; <a href="http://abelian.org/tssp/">http://abelian.org/tssp/</a>. The language test using Guess-Language package will detect the language as French which is wrong. However, when we extract more text the result will be English.&nbsp;
In order to determine the language of short text you need to install <a href="http://pythonhosted.org/pyenchant/tutorial.html#installing-pyenchant">Pyenchant</a> and other dictionaries. By default it only supports three languages: English, French, and 
Esperanto. You need to download any additional language dictionary you may need.
<br />

<br />

<span style="color: blue;">Python-Language Detector (languageIdentifier):</span><br />
Jeffrey Graves built a very light weight tool in C++ based on language hashes and wrapped in python. This tool is called <a href="http://github.com/decultured/Python-Language-Detector">Python-Language Detector</a>. It is very simple and effective. It <a href="https://github.com/decultured/Python-Language-Detector/tree/master/trigrams">detects 58 languages</a>.<br />

<br/>For example:<br />
<p class="solid">
python<br />
import languageIdentifier<br />
languageIdentifier.load(&#8220;decultured-Python-Language-Detector-edc8cfd/trigrams/&#8221;)<br />
languageIdentifier.identify(&#8220;Tesla Secondary Simulation Project&#8221;,300,300)<br />
&#8217;fr&#8217;<br />
languageIdentifier.identify("Home Page An Internet collaboration exploring the physics of Tesla resonatorsThis project was started in May 2000 with the aim of exploring the fascinating physics of self-resonant single-layer solenoids as used for Tesla transformer secondaries.We maintain a precision software model of the solenoid which is based on our best theoretical understanding of the physics. This model provides a platform for theoretical work and virtual experiments.&#8221;,300,300)<br /><br/>

&#8217;en&#8217;<br />
</p>

Here, we also noticed that the length of the text affects the result. When the text was short we falsely got "French" as the language. However, when we add more text from the webpage the correct answer appeared.<br/><br/>
Another example where we check the title of a Korean webpage, which was selected randomly from the DMOZ Korean webpage directory.<br />

<br/>For example:<br />

<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a href="http://bada.ebn.co.kr/" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-z6V6h8smkaA/VuYQtktKtxI/AAAAAAAAAL8/SF1uAUho3MkesXfBEd7T_wpCEFB7d6pNg/s320/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-03-13%2Bat%2B9.15.13%2BPM.png" /></a></div>

<p class="solid">
curl --silent http://bada.ebn.co.kr/ | grep "title"|sed -e 's/&lt;[^&gt;]*&gt;//g'<br /><br/>

EBN 물류&amp;조선 뉴스<br />
</p>

<p class="solid">
python<br />
import languageIdentifier<br />
languageIdentifier.load(&#8220;decultured-Python-Language-Detector-edc8cfd/trigrams/&#8221;)<br />
languageIdentifier.identify(&#8220;EBN 물류&amp;조선 뉴스&#8221;,300,300)<br /><br/>

&#8217;ko&#8217;<br />
</p>

Here the correct answer showed up &#8220;Korean&#8221;, although some English letters were in the title.<br />

<br />

<span style="color: blue;">LangID:</span><br />
The other tool is <a href="https://github.com/saffsd/langid.py">LangID</a>. This tool can <a href="https://github.com/saffsd/langid.py/blob/master/README.rst">detects 97 different languages</a>. As an output it&nbsp; states the confidence score for the probability prediction. The scores are re-normalized and it produces an output in the 0-1 range. This tool is one of my favorite language detection tools because it is fast, detects short texts and gives you a confidence score.<br />

<br/>For example:<br />
<p class="solid">
python<br />
import langid<br />
langid.classify(&#8220;Tesla Secondary Simulation Project&#8221;)<br /><br/>

(&#8216;en&#8217;, 0.9916567142572572)<br />
</p>

<p class="solid">
python<br />
import langid<br />
langid.classify("Home Page An Internet collaboration exploring the physics of Tesla resonatorsThis project was started in May 2000 with the aim of exploring the fascinating physics of self-resonant single-layer solenoids as used for Tesla transformer secondaries.We maintain a precision software model of the solenoid which is based on our best theoretical understanding of the physics. This model provides a platform for theoretical work and virtual experiments.")<br /><br/>

(&#8216;en&#8217;, 1.0)<br />
</p>

Using the same text above. This tool identified a small text correctly, with a confidence rate of 0.99. And when full text is provided the confidence score was 1.0.<br/>
<br/>

<br/>For example:<br />
<p class="solid">
python<br />
import langid<br />
langid.classify(&#8220;السلام عليكم ورحمة الله وبركاته&#8221;)<br /><br/>
(&#8216;ar&#8217;, 0.9999999797315073)<br />
</p>

By testing other language such as an Arabic phrase, it had a 0.99 confidence score for Arabic language.<br />

<br />

<span style="color: blue;">Google Language Detection API:</span><br />
<a href="https://detectlanguage.com">The Google Language Detection API</a> <a href="http://detectlanguage.com/languages">detects 160 different languages</a>. I have tried this tool and I think it is one of the strongest tools found. The tool can be downloaded in different programming languages: ruby, java, python, php, crystal, C#. To use this tool you have to download an API key after <a href="https://detectlanguage.com/users/sign_up">creating an account and signing-up</a>.
The language tests results in three outputs: isReliable (true, false), confidence (rate), language (language code). The tool's website mentions that the confidence rate is not a range and can be <a href="https://detectlanguage.com/faq">higher than 100</a>, no further explanation of how this score is calculated was mentioned. The API&nbsp; allows 5000 free requests a day (1 MB/day) free requests. If you need more than that there are different payable plans you can <a href="https://detectlanguage.com/plans">sign-up</a> for. You can also detect text language in an online <a href="https://detectlanguage.com/">demo</a>. I recommend this tool if you have a small data set, but it needs time to set-up and to figure out how it runs.<br />

<br/>For example:<br />

<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a href="http://moheet.com/" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-SxKLnDuzGuM/VuYREeDtB7I/AAAAAAAAAME/5hRdf1Oi5GwOQaarqxoVyjb3_SJ4AsglQ/s320/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-03-13%2Bat%2B9.16.42%2BPM.png" /></a></div>

<p class="solid">
curl --silent http://moheet.com/ | grep "title"| sed -e 's/&lt;[^&gt;]* &gt; //g' &gt; moheet.txt<br /></br>
</p>
<p class="solid">
python<br />
file1=open(&#8220;moheet.txt&#8221;,&#8221;r&#8221;)<br />
import detectlanguage<br />
detectlanguage.configuration.api_key=&#8220;Your key&#8221;<br />
detectlanguage.detect(file1)<br />
[{&#8216;isReliable&#8217;: True, &#8216;confidence&#8217;: 7.73, &#8216;language&#8217;: &#8216;ar&#8217;}]<br />
</p>

In this example, I extract text from an Arabic webpage from DMOZ Arabic Directory. The tool detected its language Arabic with True reliability and a confidence of 7.73. Note you have to remove the new line from the text so it doesn&#8217;t consider it a batch detection and give you result for each line.<br />

<br />

<span style="color: blue;">In Conclusion:</span><br />
So before you start looking for the right tool you have to determine a couple of things first:<br />
<ul>
<li>Are you trying to detect the language of a webpage or some text?<br /></li>
<li>What is the length of the text? usually more text is better and gives more accurate results (check this article on the effect of short texts on language detection: <a href="http://lab.hypotheses.org/1083">http://lab.hypotheses.org/1083</a>)<br /></li>
<li>What is the language you want to determine (if it is known or expected), because certain tools determine certain languages<br /></li>
<li> What programming language do you want to use?</li>
</ul>
<br />
Here is a short summary of the language detection methods I reviewed and a small description of all:<br />
<br />

    <td>not always found and sometimes affected by browser setting.</td>
  </tr>

    <td>fast, easy to use</td>  
    <td>works better on longer text.</td>
  </tr>

  <tr>

    <td>fast, easy to use</td>  
    <td>works better on longer text.</td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td><a href="https://github.com/saffsd/langid.py">LangID</a></td>
    <td>fast, gives you confidence score</td>  
    <td>works on both long and short text.</td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td><a href="https://detectlanguage.com">Google Language Detection API</a></td>

<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
Between March 3 - March 5, 2016, Librarians, Archivists, Historians, Computer Scientists, etc., came together for the <a href="https://artsweb.uwaterloo.ca/archivesunleashed/" target="_blank">Archives Unleashed Web Archive Hackathon</a> at the University of Toronto Robarts Library, Toronto, Ontario Canada. This event gave researchers the opportunity to collaboratively develop open-source tools for web archives. The event was organized by <a href="https://ianmilligan.ca/introduction/" target="_blank">Ian Milligan</a>, (assistant professor of Canadian and digital history in the Department of History at the University of Waterloo), <a href="http://nathaliecasemajor.com/" target="_blank">Nathalie Casemajor</a> (assistant professor in communication studies in the Department of Social Sciences at the University of Québec in Outaouais (Canada)), <a href="https://cs.uwaterloo.ca/~jimmylin/" target="_blank">Jimmy Lin</a> (the David R. Cheriton Chair in the David R. Cheriton School of Computer Science at the University of Waterloo), <a href="https://comminfo.rutgers.edu/directory/mweber/index.html" target="_blank">Matthew Weber</a> (Assistant Professor in the School of Communication and Information at Rutgers University), and <a href="https://onesearch.library.utoronto.ca/library-staff/nich-worby" target="_blank">Nicholas Worby</a> (the Government Information &amp; Statistics Librarian at the University of Toronto&#8217;s Robarts Library).<br />
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
Additionally, the event was made possible due to the support of the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council of Canada, the National Science Foundation, the University of Waterloo, the <a href="https://www.utoronto.ca/" target="_blank">University of Toronto</a>, Rutgers University, the University of Québec in Outaouais, the Internet Archive, Library and Archives Canada, and Compute Canada.&nbsp;<a href="http://www.cs.odu.edu/~salam/" target="_blank">Sawood&nbsp;Alam</a>, <a href="http://www.cs.odu.edu/~mkelly/" target="_blank">Mat Kelly</a> and <a href="http://www.cs.odu.edu/~anwala/" target="_blank">myself</a>, joined <a href="https://artsweb.uwaterloo.ca/archivesunleashed/participants/" target="_blank">researchers</a> from Europe and North America to exchange ideas in efforts to unleash our web archives. The event was split across three days.<br />

<a href="https://github.com/oduwsdl/ipwb" target="_blank"><b>Interplanetary WayBack</b></a> (<a href="http://www.cs.odu.edu/~salam/" target="_blank">Sawood&nbsp;Alam</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a href="http://www.cs.odu.edu/~mkelly/" target="_blank">Mat Kelly</a>)<br />
<br />
"Who will archive the archives?"<br />
<br />
To answer this question Sawood Alam and Mat Kelly presented the archiving and replay system called <a href="https://github.com/oduwsdl/ipwb" target="_blank">Interplanetary Wayback (ipwb)</a>. In a nutshell, during the indexing process <a href="https://github.com/oduwsdl/ipwb" target="_blank">ipwb</a> consumes WARC files one record a time, splits the record into headers and payload, pushes the two pieces into the IPFS (a peer&#8208;to&#8208;peer file system) network for persistent storage, and stores the references (digests) into to file format called CDXJ along with some other lookup keys and metadata. For replay it it finds the records in the index file and builds the response by assembling headers and payload retrieved from the IPFS network and performing necessary rewrites. The major benefits of this system include deduplication, redundancy, and shared open access.<br />
<br />
<b>Surveillance of First Nations</b> (<a href="http://actproject.ca/act/evan-light-2/" target="_blank">Evan Light</a>, <a href="http://york.academia.edu/KatherineCook" target="_blank">Katherine Cook</a>, <a href="https://www.library.ualberta.ca/staff/todd-suomela" target="_blank">Todd Suomela</a>, and <a href="http://www2.hawaii.edu/~rrath/" target="_blank">Richard Rath</a>)<br />
<br />
<a href="https://docs.google.com/presentation/d/1S1d0NwQYjGo4WFbhwgHJ4RLKymB41obfpe-gqhJm3Mw/pub?start=false&amp;loop=false&amp;delayms=3000&amp;slide=id.p" target="_blank"><b>Nuage</b></a> (<a href="https://ufal.mff.cuni.cz/petra-galuscakova" target="_blank">Petra Galuscakova</a>, <a href="https://www.mun.ca/geog/people/faculty/ngupta.php" target="_blank">Neha Gupta</a>, Rosa Iris R. Rovira,&nbsp;<a href="http://nathaliecasemajor.com/" target="_blank">Nathalie Casemajor</a>,&nbsp;<a href="https://about.me/srocheleau" target="_blank">Sylvain Rocheleau</a>, <a href="https://ca.linkedin.com/in/ryandeschamps?trk=seokp-title_posts_secondary_cluster_res_author_name" target="_blank">Ryan&nbsp;Deschamps</a>,&nbsp;and <a href="http://annenberg.usc.edu/faculty/doctoral-students/ruqin-ren" target="_blank">Ruqin Ren</a>)<br />
<br />
<b>Graph&#8208;X&#8208;Graphics</b> (<a href="https://uwaterloo.ca/web-archive-group/people-profiles/jeremy-wiebe" target="_blank">Jeremy Wiebe</a>, <a href="http://pain.lab.yorku.ca/eric-oosenbrug/" target="_blank">Eric Oosenbrug</a>, and <a href="https://psyborgs.github.io/" target="_blank">Shane Martin</a>)<br />
<br />
<a href="http://www.slideshare.net/anwala/tracking-discourse-on-social-media" target="_blank">Tracking Discourse in Social Media</a> (<a href="https://twitter.com/smythbound" target="_blank">Tom Smyth</a>, <a href="https://twitter.com/AllisonHegel" target="_blank">Allison Hegel</a>, <a href="http://www.cs.odu.edu/~anwala/" target="_blank">Alexander Nwala</a>, <a href="http://ucc-ie.academia.edu/PatrickEgan" target="_blank">Patrick Egan</a>,&nbsp;<a href="http://ruebot.net/" target="_blank">Nick Ruest</a>,&nbsp;<a href="http://annenberg.usc.edu/faculty/doctoral-students/yu-xu" target="_blank">Yu Xu</a>, <a href="http://cornell.academia.edu/KelseyUtne" target="_blank">Kelsey Utne</a>,&nbsp;<a href="https://twitter.com/jonathangrams" target="_blank">Jonathan Armoza</a>, and&nbsp;<a href="https://twitter.com/f_nanni" target="_blank">Federico Nanni</a>)<br />
<a href="https://www.blogger.com/"></a><span id="goog_610202704"></span><span id="goog_610202705"></span><br />
This team processed ~11.2 million tweets and ~50 million reddit comments which referenced the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Hebdo_shooting" target="_blank">Charlie Hebdo</a> and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/November_2015_Paris_attacks#Bataclan_theatre_massacre" target="_blank">Bataclan</a>&nbsp;attacks, in an effort to track the evolution of social media commentary about the attacks. The team sought to measure the attention span, information/misinformation flow, as well as the co-occurence network of terms in order to understand the dynamics of commentary about these events.<br />

The votes were tallied and&nbsp;<a href="https://docs.google.com/presentation/d/1S1d0NwQYjGo4WFbhwgHJ4RLKymB41obfpe-gqhJm3Mw/pub?start=false&amp;loop=false&amp;delayms=3000&amp;slide=id.p" target="_blank">Nuage</a>&nbsp;team got the most votes, and were declared winners. <div class='post hentry uncustomized-post-template' itemprop='blogPost' itemscope='itemscope' itemtype='http://schema.org/BlogPosting'>
<meta content='https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-NFbNxucRJUY/VqmQm7KDRzI/AAAAAAAAAPk/9FytRIMzzGc/s400/cove_landsat.png' itemprop='image_url'/>

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://ws-dl.blogspot.com/2016/03/2016-03-07-custom-missions-in-cove-tool.html'>2016-03-07: Custom Missions in the COVE Tool</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-2883607791509789573' itemprop='description articleBody'>
When I am not studying Web Sciences at ODU, I work as a software developer at <a href="http://www.ama-inc.com/">Analytical Mechanics Associates</a>. In general, my work there aims to make satellite data more accessible. As part of this mission, one of my primary projects is the <a href="http://ceos-cove.org/index.php/covetool/">COVE tool</a>.<br />
<br />
<table align="center" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; text-align: center;"><tbody>
<tr><td style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-NFbNxucRJUY/VqmQm7KDRzI/AAAAAAAAAPk/9FytRIMzzGc/s1600/cove_landsat.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"><img border="0" height="205" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-NFbNxucRJUY/VqmQm7KDRzI/AAAAAAAAAPk/9FytRIMzzGc/s400/cove_landsat.png" width="400" /></a></td></tr>
<tr><td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">The COVE tool allows a user to view where a satellite could potentially take an image. The above image shows the ground swath of both Landsat 7 (red) and Landsat 8 (green) over a one day period.&nbsp;</td></tr>
</tbody></table>
The <a href="http://ceos.org/">CEOS</a> Visualization Environment (COVE) tool is a browser-based system that leverages <a href="https://cesiumjs.org/">Cesium</a>, an open-source JavaScript library for 3D globes and maps, in order to display satellite sensor coverage areas and identify coincidence scene locations. In other words, the COVE tool allows the user to see where a satellite could potentially take an image and where two or more satellite paths overlap during a specified time period. The Committee on Earth Observing Satellites (CEOS) is currently operating and planning hundreds of Earth observation satellites. &nbsp;COVE initially began as a way to improve Standard Calibration and Validation (Cal/Val) exercises for these satellites. Cal/Val exercises need to compare near-simultaneous surface observations and identify corresponding image pairs in order to calibrate and validate the satellite's orbit. These tasks are time-consuming and labor-intensive. The COVE tool has been pivotal in making these Cal/Val exercises much easier and more efficient.<br />
<br />
<table align="center" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; text-align: center;"><tbody>
<tr><td style="text-align: center;"><a href="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-7W-ujWJn93o/Vrx5rMuy8nI/AAAAAAAAAQI/8e-8N4Mar74/s1600/cove_coincidence.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"><img border="0" height="205" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-7W-ujWJn93o/Vrx5rMuy8nI/AAAAAAAAAQI/8e-8N4Mar74/s400/cove_coincidence.png" width="400" /></a></td></tr>
<tr><td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">The COVE tool allows a user to see possible coincidences of two satellites. The above image shows the coincidences of ALOS-2 with Landsat 7 over a one week period.</td></tr>
</tbody></table>
In the past, the COVE tool only allowed for this analysis to be done on historical, operational, or notional satellite missions with known orbit data, which COVE could then use to predict the propagation of the orbit accurately, within the bounds of the model&#8217;s assumptions, for up to three (3) months passed the last-known orbit data. This has proven extremely useful for those missions that the orbit data is known; however, it was limited to these missions.<br />
<br />
Mission planning is another task which includes the prediction of satellite orbits, a task the COVE tool was well equipped for. However, in mission planning exercises, the orbit data of the satellite is unknown. Based on this need, we wanted to extend COVE to include customized missions, in which the user could define the orbit parameters and the COVE tool would then predict the orbit of the customized mission through a numerical propagation. I had the opportunity to be the lead developer for this new feature, which recently went live and can be accessed through the Custom Missions tab on the right of the COVE tool, as shown in the video below. This is an important addition to the COVE tool, as it allows for better planning of potential future missions and will hopefully help to improve satellite coverage of Earth in the future.<br />

Video Summary:<br />
<b>00:07:04 </b>- The "Custom" Missions and Instruments tab shows a list of the current user's custom missions. Currently, we do not have any custom missions.<br />
<b>00:09:03</b> - To create a custom mission, choose "Custom Missions" on the right panel. First, we need to "Add Mission." Once we have a mission we can add additional instruments to the instrument or delete the mission.<br />
<b>00:20:15</b> - After choosing a mission name, we need to decide if we want to use an existing mission's orbit or define a custom orbit. We want to create a custom orbit. Clicking on "Custom defined orbit" gives three more options. A circular orbit is the most basic and for the novice user. A repeating sun synchronous orbit is a subset of circular orbits that must cover each area around the same time. For example, if the satellite passes over Hampton, VA at 10:00 AM, its next pass over Hampton should also be at 10:00 AM. The advanced orbit is for the experienced user and allows full control over the orbital parameters. We will create a repeating sun synchronous orbit, similar to Landsat 8.<br />
<b>00:33:14</b> - When creating a repeating sun synchronous orbit, the altitude given is only an estimate as only certain inclination/altitude pairs are able to repeat. Thus, the user has the option to calculate the inclination and altitude that will be used.<br />
<b>00:37:24</b> - The instrument and mode, along with the altitude of the orbit we just defined, determine the swath size of the potential images the satellite will be able to take.<br />
<b>00:49:23</b> - We need to define "Field of View" and "Pointing Angle" of the instrument. We will also choose "Daylight only," our custom mission will only take images during the daylight hours. This is useful because many optical satellites, such as Landsat 8 are "Daylight only" since they cannot take good optical images at night.<br />
<b>01:02:06</b> - We will now choose a date range over which we will propagate the orbit to see what our satellite's path will look like.<br />
<b>01:21:18</b> - We can now see what path our satellite will take during the daylight hours, since we chose "Daylight only."<br />

<a href='http://ws-dl.blogspot.com/2016/02/2016-02-24-acquisition-of-mementos-and.html'>2016-02-24: Acquisition of Mementos and Their Content Is More Challenging Than Expected</a>

<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-4670123550143927046' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<a href="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-nhePShubmJE/VsuMjwssL4I/AAAAAAAACZ4/754RvBp1vwo/s1600/proni-page.png" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;"><img border="0" height="226" src="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-nhePShubmJE/VsuMjwssL4I/AAAAAAAACZ4/754RvBp1vwo/s320/proni-page.png" width="320" /></a>Recently, we conducted an experiment using mementos for almost 700,000 web pages from more than 20 web archives. &nbsp;These web pages spanned much of the life of the web (1997-2012). Much has been written about acquiring and extracting text from live web pages, but we believe that this is an unparalleled attempted to acquire and extract text from mementos themselves. Our experiment is also distinct from<br />
<a href="http://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007%2F978-3-319-24592-8_17">AlNoamany's work</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a href="http://anjackson.net/2015/04/27/what-have-we-saved-iipc-ga-2015/">Andy Jackson's work</a>, because we are trying to acquire and extract text from mementos across many web archives, rather than just one.<br />
<br />
We initially expected the acquisition and text extraction of mementos to be a relatively simple exercise, but quickly discovered that the idiosyncrasies between web archives made these operations much more complex. &nbsp;We document our findings in a technical report entitled: &nbsp;"<a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/1602.06223">Rules of Acquisition for Mementos and Their Content</a>".<br />
<br />
Our technical report briefly covers the following key points:<br />
<ul>
<li>Special techniques for acquiring mementos from the WebCite on-demand archive (<a href="http://www.webcitation.org/">http://www.webcitation.org</a>)</li>
<li>Special techniques for dealing with JavaScript Redirects created by the Internet Archive</li>
<li>An alternative to <a href="http://www.crummy.com/software/BeautifulSoup/">BeautifulSoup</a> for removing elements and extracting text from mementos</li>
<li>Stripping away archive-specific additions to memento content</li>
<li>An algorithm for dealing with inaccurate character encoding</li>
<li>Differences in whitespace treatment between archives for the same archived page</li>
<li>Control characters in HTML and their effect on DOM parsers</li>
<li>DOM-corruption in various HTML pages exacerbated by how the archives present the text stored within &lt;noscript&gt; elements</li>
</ul>
<div>
Rather than repeating the entire technical report here, we want to focus on the two issues of interest that may have the greater impact on others acquiring and experimenting with mementos: acquiring mementos from Web Cite and inaccurate character encoding.</div>
<div>
<h2>
Acquisition of Content from WebCite</h2>
<br />
WebCite is an on-demand archive specializing in archiving web pages used as citations in scholarly work. &nbsp;An example WebCite page is shown below.<br />
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-zCjYnF11Ags/VstXeKJ_QZI/AAAAAAAACZg/ctKCtsNj0lE/s1600/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-02-22%2Bat%2B11.45.55%2BAM.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="400" src="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-zCjYnF11Ags/VstXeKJ_QZI/AAAAAAAACZg/ctKCtsNj0lE/s400/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-02-22%2Bat%2B11.45.55%2BAM.png" width="371" /></a></div>
For acquiring most memento content, we utilized the <a href="https://curl.haxx.se/">cURL data transfer tool</a>. &nbsp;With this tool, one merely types the following command to save the contents of the URI http://www.example.com:</div>
<div>
<br /></div>
<div>
<pre>curl -o outputfile.html http://www.example.com
</pre>
</div>
<div>
<br /></div>
<div>
For WebCite, the output from cURL for a given URI-M results in the same HTML frameset content, regardless of which URI-M is used. &nbsp;We sought to acquire the actual content of a given page for text extraction, so merely utilizing cURL was insufficient. &nbsp;An example of this HTML is shown below.</div>
<div>
<br /></div>
<div>
<textarea cols="60" rows="21"><!DOCTYPE html PUBLIC " -//W3C//DTD XHTML 1.0 Frameset //EN" "http :// www.w3.org/TR/xhtml1/
DTD/xhtml1 -frameset.dtd">
<html xmlns="http :// www.w3.org /1999/ xhtml" xml : lang="en" lang="en">
<head>
<meta http&#8722;equiv="Content -Type" content="text/html; charset=utf -8"/>
<title>WebCite query result</ title>
<link rel="stylesheet" type="text/css" href="/basic.css" />
<link rel="stylesheet" type="text/css" href="/nicetitle.css" />
<script src="https://www.google.com/recaptcha/api.js" async defer></ script>
</head>
<frameset rows="60 ,*" frameborder="0">
<frame src="./topframe.php" name="nav" noresize="noresize" marginwidth="0" marginheight="0" scrolling="no" />
<frame src="./mainframe.php" name="main" noresize="noresize" marginwidth="0" marginheight="0" />
</frameset>
</html>
</textarea>
</div>
<div>
<br /></div>
<div>
Instead of relying on cURL, we analyzed the resulting HTML frameset and determined that the content is actually returned by a request to the mainframe.php file. &nbsp;Unfortunately, merely issuing a request to the mainframe.php file is insufficient because the cookies sent to the browser indicate which memento should be displayed. We developed custom PhantomJS code, presented as Listing 1 in <a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/1602.06223">the technical report</a>, for overcoming this issue. &nbsp;PhatomJS, because it must acquire, parse, and process the content of a page, is <a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/1508.02315">much slower</a> than merely using cURL.<br />
<br />
The requirement to utilize a web browser, rather than HTTP only, for the acquisition of web content is common for live web content, as detailed by <a href="http://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-642-40501-3_5">Kelly</a> and <a href="http://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00799-015-0140-8">Brunelle</a>, but we did not anticipate that we would need a browser simulation tool, such as PhantomJS, to acquire memento content.<br />
<br />
In addition to the issue of acquiring mementos, we also discovered reliability problems with Web Cite, seen in the figure below. &nbsp;We would routinely need to reattempt downloads of the same URI-M in order to finally acquire its content.<br />
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-ozGKitEHdJQ/VstWTUh5ohI/AAAAAAAACZU/JC02L-b9vSs/s1600/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-02-22%2Bat%2B11.41.01%2BAM.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="400" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-ozGKitEHdJQ/VstWTUh5ohI/AAAAAAAACZU/JC02L-b9vSs/s400/Screen%2BShot%2B2016-02-22%2Bat%2B11.41.01%2BAM.png" width="371" /></a></div>
<br />
Finally, we experienced rate limiting from Web Cite, forcing us to divide our list of URI-Ms and download content from several source networks.<br />
<br />
Because of these issues, the acquisition of almost 100,000 mementos from Web Cite took more than 1 month to complete, compared to the acquisition of 1 million mementos from the Internet Archive in 2 weeks.<br />
<br />
<h2>
Inaccurate Character Encoding</h2>
<br />
Extracting text from documents requires that such text be decoded properly for processes such as text similarity or topic analysis. &nbsp; For a subset of mementos, some archives do not present the correct <a href="http://www.iana.org/assignments/character-sets/character-sets.xhtml">character set</a>&nbsp;in <a href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc7231#section-3.1.1.5">the HTTP Content-Type header</a>. &nbsp;Even though&nbsp;<a href="https://www.blogger.com/6https://googleblog.blogspot.com/2008/05/movingto-unicode-51.html">most web sites now use the UTF-8 character set</a>, a subset of our mementos come from a time before UTF-8 was adopted so proper decoding becomes an issue.<br />
<br />
To address this issue, we developed a simple algorithm that attempts to detect and use the character encoding for a given document.<br />
<br />
<ol>
<li>Use the character set from the HTTP Content-Type header, if present; otherwise try UTF-8.</li>
<li>If a character encoding is discovered in the file contents, as is common for XHTML documents, then try to use that; otherwise try UTF-8.</li>
<li>If any of the character sets encountered raise an error, raise our own error.</li>
</ol>
<br />
We fall back to UTF-8 because it is <a href="http://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc3629">an effective superset</a> of many of the character sets for the mementos in our collection, such as ASCII. This algorithm worked for more than 99% of our dataset.<br />
<br />
In the future, we intend to explore the use of <a href="http://userguide.icu-project.org/conversion/%20detection">confidence-based tools</a>, such as the <a href="https://pypi.python.org/pypi/chardet">chardet library</a>,&nbsp;to guess the character set when extracting text. &nbsp;The use of such tools takes more time than merely using the Content-Type header, but are necessary when that header is unreliable and algorithms such as ours fail.<br />
<br />
<h2>
Summary</h2>
<br />
We were able to overcome most of the memento acquisition and text extraction issues encountered in our experiment. &nbsp;Because we were unaware of the problems we would encounter, we felt that it would be useful to <a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/1602.06223">detail our solutions for others</a>&nbsp;to assist them in their own research and engineering.<br />

<div class='post hentry uncustomized-post-template' itemprop='blogPost' itemscope='itemscope' itemtype='http://schema.org/BlogPosting'>
<meta content='http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-3nDaBX1LW5o/VqJRg8WeLwI/AAAAAAAAAtk/_uqNc7YhUdc/s640/24393571956_94c73f197c_o.jpg' itemprop='image_url'/>

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://ws-dl.blogspot.com/2016/01/2016-01-xx-january-2016-federal-cloud.html'>2016-01-28: January 2016 Federal Cloud Computing Summit</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-1991898574665200946' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<div style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<span style="font-family: inherit; margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-3nDaBX1LW5o/VqJRg8WeLwI/AAAAAAAAAtk/_uqNc7YhUdc/s1600/24393571956_94c73f197c_o.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="88" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-3nDaBX1LW5o/VqJRg8WeLwI/AAAAAAAAAtk/_uqNc7YhUdc/s640/24393571956_94c73f197c_o.jpg" width="640" /></a></span></div>
<div style="text-align: left;">
<span style="font-family: inherit; margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><span style="font-family: inherit;"><br /></span></span></div>
<div style="text-align: left;">
<span style="font-family: &quot;calibri&quot; , &quot;arial&quot; , &quot;helvetica&quot; , sans-serif; font-size: 16px;"><span style="font-family: &quot;calibri&quot; , &quot;arial&quot; , &quot;helvetica&quot; , sans-serif; font-size: 16px;"><span style="font-family: &quot;calibri&quot; , &quot;arial&quot; , &quot;helvetica&quot; , sans-serif; font-size: 16px;">As I have <a href="http://ws-dl.blogspot.com/2015/01/2015-01-15-winter-2015-federal-cloud.html">mentioned previously</a>, I am the </span><a href="http://www.mitre.org/" style="font-family: Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: 16px;">MITRE</a><span style="font-family: &quot;calibri&quot; , &quot;arial&quot; , &quot;helvetica&quot; , sans-serif;"> chair of the </span><a href="http://www.fedsummits.com/cloud/" style="font-family: Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: 16px;">Federal Cloud Computing summit</a><span style="font-family: &quot;calibri&quot; , &quot;arial&quot; , &quot;helvetica&quot; , sans-serif;">. The Summits are designed to allow representatives from government agencies that would not necessarily cross paths to collaborate and learn from one another about the best practices, challenges, and recommendations for adopting emerging technologies in the federal government. The MITRE-</span><a href="http://www.atarc.org/" style="font-family: Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: 16px;">ATARC</a><span style="font-family: &quot;calibri&quot; , &quot;arial&quot; , &quot;helvetica&quot; , sans-serif;"> Collaboration Symposium is a working group-style session in which academics, representatives from industry, government, and <a href="http://www.mitre.org/publications/all/understanding-federally-funded-research-and-development-centers-ffrdcs">FFRDC</a> representatives discuss potential solutions and ways-forward for the top challenges of emerging technology adoption in government. MITRE helps select the challenge areas by polling government practitioners on their top challenges, and the participants break into groups to discuss each challenge area. The Collaboration Symposium allows this&nbsp;heterogeneous&nbsp;group of&nbsp;cloud practitioners to collaborate across all levels, from the end users to researchers to practitioners to policy makers (at the officer level).</span></span></span></div>
<br />
<span style="font-family: inherit;">
</span>
<span style="font-family: inherit;"><br /></span>
<a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-8mI977TbRS8/VqJRoHJ-IpI/AAAAAAAAAts/3Dpsn9YtWJU/s1600/23792997003_85cebe7228_o.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><span style="font-family: inherit;"><img border="0" height="320" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-8mI977TbRS8/VqJRoHJ-IpI/AAAAAAAAAts/3Dpsn9YtWJU/s320/23792997003_85cebe7228_o.jpg" width="320" /></span></a><br />
<br />
<br />
<span style="font-family: &quot;calibri&quot; , &quot;arial&quot; , &quot;helvetica&quot; , sans-serif; font-size: 16px;">The Summit series includes </span><a href="http://www.fedsummits.com/mobile/">mobile</a><span style="background-color: white;">,</span><a href="http://www.fedsummits.com/ioe/"> Internet of Everything</a><span style="background-color: white;">, </span><a href="http://www.fedsummits.com/big-data/">big data</a><span style="background-color: white;">, and </span><a href="http://www.fedsummits.com/security/">cyber security</a><span style="background-color: white;"> summits along with the cloud summit, each of which occurs twice each year. MITRE produces a white paper that summarizes the MITRE-ATARC Collaboration Symposium. The white paper is shared with industry to communicate the top challenges and current needs of the federal government to guide product development, academia to identify the skillsets needed by the government and influence curricula development along with research topics, and government to communicate best practices and current challenges of other peer government agencies.</span>

<br />
<span style="font-family: inherit;"><br style="font-family: Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: 16px;" /></span>
<span style="background-color: white;"><span style="font-family: inherit;">The Summit takes place in Washington, D.C. and is a full-day event. The day begins at 7:30 AM with registration and an industry trade show that allows industry representatives to communicate with government representatives about their challenges and the solutions that industry has to offer. At 9:00, a series of panel discussions by academic researchers and government. This also allows audience members to ask questions to the top&nbsp;implementers&nbsp;of cloud&nbsp;computing in the government and academia.</span></span><br />
<span style="font-family: inherit;"><br style="font-family: Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: 16px;" /></span>
<span style="background-color: white; font-family: inherit;">At 1:15, after lunch, the MITRE-ATARC Collaboration Symposium begins, and runs until 3:45. There is also a final out-briefing from each collaboration session a teh end of the day to communicate the major findings from each session to the summit participants.</span><br />
<span style="font-family: inherit;"><br style="font-family: Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: 16px;" /></span>
<span style="background-color: white; font-family: inherit;">Common threads from the summit included the importance of cloud security, the importance of incorporating other emerging technologies (e.g., mobile, big data, Internet of Things) in cloud computing, and how each emerging technology enables or enhances the others, and the importance of agile processes in cloud migration planning. More details on the outcomes will be included in the white paper, which should be released in 6-8 weeks. Prior white papers are available at the <a href="http://www.atarc.org/documents/">ATARC website</a>.</span><br />
<span style="font-family: inherit;"><br style="font-family: Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: 16px;" /></span>
<span style="background-color: white; font-family: inherit;">The results of the Summit has implications for web archivists. With the increasing importance and emphasis on mobile, IoT, and cloud services, particularly within the government, there is an increased importance on archiving representations and the use of this material. As Julie Brill mentioned in her <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_yB80xD3xvY">CNI talk</a>, the government is interested in understanding how these services and technologies are being used regardless of whether or not there is a UI or other interface with which humans can interact.&nbsp;</span><br />
<span style="font-family: inherit;"><br style="font-family: Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: 16px;" /></span>
<span style="background-color: white; font-family: inherit;">Archiving data endpoints from HTTP is comparatively trivial (although challenges still exist with archiving at high fidelity, particularly when considering JavaScript and deferred representations), but archiving a data service that might exchange data through non-HTTP or even push (as opposed to pull) transactions may change the paradigm used for web archiving.</span><br />
<span style="font-family: inherit;"><br style="font-family: Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: 16px;" /></span>
<span style="background-color: white; font-family: inherit;">With increased adoption, the archiving of representations reliant or designed to be consumed through emerging technologies will continue to increase and highlights a potential frontier in web archiving and digital preservation.</span><br />
<span style="font-family: inherit;"><br style="font-family: Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: 16px;" /></span>
<span style="font-family: inherit;"><br style="font-family: Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; font-size: 16px;" /></span>
<span style="background-color: white; font-family: inherit;">--<a href="http://www.justinfbrunelle/">Justin F. Brunelle</a>&nbsp;<span style="font-size: xx-small;">*</span></span><br />
<span style="background-color: white; font-family: inherit;"><br /></span>
