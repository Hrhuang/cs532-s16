                                <p>Fifty years ago today, St. Louis got its foot in the door with the National Hockey League, gaining a conditional expansion franchise from the leagues Board of Governors.</p><p>The Blues birth was unconventional, like much of the teams subsequent history. Chicago Blackhawks owners James D. Norris and William Wirtz also owned the St. Louis Arena and wanted to sell. They saw the leagues expansion from six to 12 teams as their way to get value.</p><p>So what if nobody here wanted a team? That small detail could be addressed later. Norris and Wirtz had pull. St. Louis got the NHL nod, over Baltimore, pending the recruitment of an owner.</p><!-- [1723] oSettings.url: /sports/columns/jeff-gordon --><div class="tnt-ads-container text-center hidden-sm hidden-md hidden-lg"><div class="tnt-ads dfp-ad" data-dfp-adunit="/8438/stltoday.com/sports/columns/jeff-gordon" data-dfp-custom-pos="fixed-big-ad-top, atf" id="fixed-big-ad-top-mobile" data-dfp-size="[300,250]"></div></div>
    <p>Sid Salomon Jr., his son Sid Salomon III and Robert Wolfson took on the challenge. They did it up right. They bought and renovated the Arena, at a cost of $6.5 million, paid the $2 million expansion fee and hired former Boston Bruins general manager Lynn Patrick.</p><p>Patrick, in turn, hired up-and-comers Scotty Bowman and Cliff Fletcher from the Montreal Canadiens to lead his talent search. He gave another future NHL GM, Jimmy Devellano, his first job in scouting.</p><p>Their leadership, combined with strong commitment from the Salomons, made the Blues a model franchise. They focused on defense and goaltending in the expansion draft, building around Jimmy Roberts, Noel Picard and Al Arbour at the blue line and Glenn Hall in the net. Subsequent deals brought rugged defensemen Bob and Barclay Plager, goal-scorer Red Berenson and another Hall of Fame goaltender, Jacques Plante.</p><p>The team gained traction once Bowman replaced Patrick as coach. He began his NHL coaching career by coaxing productive twilight years from respected veterans while giving some younger players like the Plagers their opportunity.</p><p>The good times lasted three seasons. The Blues reached the Stanley Cup Finals each time and set a high standard the franchise is still trying to meet today.</p><p>Like Sid Salomon III, Blues Chairman Tom Stillman is trying to do it up right. His hockey operation is first-rate. General manager Doug Armstrong and coach Ken Hitchcock have glittering credentials, including Stanley Cups won elsewhere.</p><p>Assistant GM Martin Brodeur and senior adviser Al MacInnis also have championship pedigrees. So does Brett Hull, who is helping over on the business side.</p><p>When the Blues square off <a href="http://www.stltoday.com/sports/hockey/professional/preview-blues-vs-jets/article_b9c5226f-a81c-5baa-83c6-a0c8ecf8fcf7.html" target="_blank">against the Winnipeg Jets on Tuesday night at Scottrade Center</a>  with Bob Plager dropping the ceremonial first puck, honoring this 50th birthday of sorts  they will be tied for fourth overall in the standings.</p><p>Their goal is the same as it was five decades ago, to bring a Cup to St. Louis. But their path to the finals is much, much tougher.</p><p>In those first three years the Blues needed only to play their way out of the expansion bracket to play for the Cup. The Original Six teams battled it out on the other side.</p><p>These days the Western Conference is much tougher, with the Chicago Blackhawks and Los Angeles Kings operating mini-dynasties. The road to the finals is fraught with danger.</p><p>Still, the qualities that separated the expansion-era Blues teams from the pack are worth emulating today. Those squads were hard to play against, featuring stellar goaltending, robust defensemen and the knack for timely goal-scoring.</p><style>

        </div><p>The Original Six powers swept them out of the Cup Finals in each visit, with Montreal beating them twice and Boston taking them out in the 1969-70 Flying Orr Series. But the Blues showed well each time, turning most games into hard-fought affairs.</p><p>What the current Blues have  and what those early teams lacked  is prime-age talent and depth. Back in the day Bowman had to resort to, say, talking the goal-scorer Dickie Moore out of retirement to lead one more playoff run.</p><p>Much happened during the decades between the glorious early days and Stillmans Cup quest. The franchise has suffered ownership collapses, managerial chaos, budget slashes, talent churn, stunningly bad luck, heartbreaking tragedy and one near-move to Saskatoon.</p><p>One of the franchises most hated owners, Harry Ornest, helped save the franchise from oblivion. His Blues also came within two goals of playing for the Cup in 1986.</p><p>One of the franchises most beloved chairmen, Mike Shanahan, operated willy-nilly. His hiring of oily-haired despot Mike Keenan set the Blues back for years.</p><!-- [1723] oSettings.url: /sports/columns/jeff-gordon --><div class="tnt-ads-container text-center hidden-sm hidden-md hidden-lg"><div class="tnt-ads dfp-ad" data-dfp-adunit="/8438/stltoday.com/sports/columns/jeff-gordon" data-dfp-custom-pos="fixed-big-ad-middle, stf" id="fixed-big-ad-middle-mobile" data-dfp-size="[300,250]"></div></div>
    <p>A billionaire, Bill Laurie, gutted the team and bailed after failing to land a NBA team to couple it with. A millionaire, Dave Checketts, stepped in and ran up huge deficits he couldnt cover.</p><p>A parade of all-time greats, including 10 of the top 40 scorers in NHL history, passed through the franchise. Most, like the great Wayne Gretzky, didnt stay long.</p><p>Future goal scorer Paul MacLean exited in a trade for defenseman Scott Campbell, who quickly retired with an asthma condition. Ornest ordered the departure of Joey Mullen and Mike Liut in salary dumps. Doug Gilmour was exiled after alleged adventures in babysitting.</p><p>Judge Edward Houston swiped Scott Stevens to punish the Blues for signing Brendan Shanahan, who was later traded for Chris Pronger amid domestic turbulence. Prongers run here ended with the much-lamented Eric Brewer trade.</p><p>Defenseman Ed Kea suffered a traumatic head injury during a game. First overall draft pick Erik Johnson wrecked his knee during golf cart high jinks.</p><p>Rugged Bob Gassoff died in a motorcycle wreck after a team event. Lynn Patrick died of a heart attack after leaving a Blues game. Broadcaster Dan Kelly and Monday Night Miracle hero Doug Wickenheiser lost their cancer battles.</p><p>Pavol Demitra and Igor Korolev died in a jet crash in Russia while traveling with their KHL team. Tough guy Todd Ewen recently took his own life.</p><p>Through it all the franchise persevered. The latest bit of Blues adversity, <a href="http://www.stltoday.com/sports/hockey/professional/pietrangelo-out-with-knee-injury/article_16172d50-4cd3-56c7-88c3-f9de97a57328.html" target="_blank">Alex Pietrangelos weeks-long absence with a knee injury</a>, seems pretty minor in the context of this decades-long struggle.</p><p>You have years like this ... weve got one, weve been able to keep this thing going taking a lot of body blows, Hitchcock said after Mondays practice. Its like OK, damage done, now lets move forward. Nobody in here is overwhelmed by whats going on. Weve had to win games missing seven, eight guys. So this is something that weve been used to all year. Weve been able to move forward emotionally, mentally, physically, everything.</p><p>Fifty years on, the Blues are still battling, still striving to take that final step.</p><!-- [1723] oSettings.url: /sports/columns/jeff-gordon --><div class="tnt-ads-container text-center hidden-sm hidden-md hidden-lg"><div class="tnt-ads dfp-ad" data-dfp-adunit="/8438/stltoday.com/sports/columns/jeff-gordon" data-dfp-custom-pos="fixed-big-ad-bottom, btf" id="fixed-big-ad-bottom-mobile" data-dfp-size="[300,250]"></div></div>
