Kellis High student with disabilities, takes a shot during the Unified Movement Special Olympics program basketball game against Sunrise High School at Raymond S. Kellis High School in Glendale on Tuesday, February 2, 2016. The Unified Movement Special Olympics program pairs students with disabilities with their typical peer students.</span><meta itemprop="copyrightHolder" content="" /><span class="credit">(Photo: David Wallace/The Republic)</span></span></p></aside></section></div></div><p>At well over 6 feet tall, Phillipo Howery doesn’t need to stand on tippy toes. He’s not like the swarm of teenagers around him who lined the campus walkway on a recent Tuesday, all of them craning their necks and climbing on benches to see over the crowd cheering for the visitors.</p><p>It’s just past noon, and the campus is filling with teachers, coaches, CEOs and athletes who came from as far as New York to see the big basketball game. One by one, they step off the tour bus, which had pulled up to Raymond S. Kellis High School on North 91st Avenue near Northern Avenue, where small farms and tract homes spread out in the western suburbs.</p><p>“Woooo! Yaaaay!” the high schoolers shout. The steady thud of bass drums is the only sound from the marching band loud enough to carry over the screaming teenagers. Visitors, stunned by the over-the-top welcome, stop to take pictures. They pose for selfies inside the gaping mouth of an inflatable tunnel that looks like a cougar’s head. Then they make their way into the school’s auditorium.</p><p>Across the hallway is the gym, where, when the pep rally ends, students will take part in a game with athletes who not so long ago wouldn't have had the chance to play.</p><p>Phillipo is watching, taking it all in. He doesn’t need to stand on his tippy toes to see the scene. But he does.</p><p>When Phillipo gets excited, he has a habit. Like the thin branches of a paloverde bending under the force of a summer wind, the lanky boy leans forward as if he might fall. He rises up, clenches his hands and laughs in shouts and bursts.</p><p>Phillipo is 15. He looks like a little boy when his eyes grow big and his mouth opens wide to holler and he waves at everyone here to see his team play.</p><p>On the basketball court, there is nothing little or boyish about Phillipo. He’s fierce. So fierce, coaches had to teach him how to control his emotions so he could focus on his game and on the reason these kids are here to play.</p><div id="module-position-O-zXv6RhxYw" class="story-asset 4463</a></li></div></div></div></div><p>He can’t wait.</p><p>But first he has to walk to the front of the auditorium, stand at the podium and give a speech in front of everyone.</p><p>He waits for the principal, a hype-man who gets the crowd going with school chants: “Cougar pride, is it in you?” Then he waits as the audience watches a video, set to a pop-music soundtrack and starring high-school athletes. Finally, all the other speakers who take the stage before Phillipo have had their turn.</p><p>He hears his teacher Michael Wakeford call his name: “Is Phillipo in the house?”</p><p>He can hear the sound of teenagers screaming for him echoing through the dark auditorium. Phillipo hurries up the steps as Wakeford is finishing his introduction.</p><p>“Phillipo Howery is a current Special Olympic athlete here at Kellis High School,” Wakeford says, raising his voice over the cheering crowd. “And you’ll see him and his abilities in the basketball game.”</p><p>Abilities. It’s a word you’ll hear over and over at Kellis, a place where teenagers have been taught that everyone is different and everyone is able.</p><p>Phillipo doesn’t know it yet, but in a few days, leaders with <a href="http://www.specialolympicsarizona.org/">Special Olympics Arizona</a> will speak at an international conference at the United Nations headquarters in Austria, spotlighting athletes like him to an audience of hundreds of dignitaries from more than 50 countries. Athletes who play in Special Olympics Unified Sports programs across the nation with one goal: Unite students with all levels of physical and developmental abilities and disabilities.</p><p>Arizona's program has been so successful it was selected as a model for inclusion, which global policymakers hope to see replicated across the world.</p><p>But right now, it’s Phillipo’s turn in the spotlight. He takes the stage. It’s his time to speak.</p><h2 class="presto-h2">Tearing down invisible barriers</h2><p>There are nearly 2,000 students enrolled at Kellis High. About half the school is economically disadvantaged. Ethnic minority enrollment is just over 61 percent and testing scores are on par with the district’s other seven schools, according to a <em>U.S. News &amp; World Report </em>analysis.</p><p>If you ask Kellis students, these are all just a bunch of numbers. What sets their school apart is how, at a time when high schools across America continue to battle bullying, teens on their campus go out of their way to show they care about each other.</p><p>If you ask teachers, the camaraderie they’ve built among Kellis students has everything to do with the <a href="http://www.specialolympics.org/projectunify.aspx">Special Olympics’ Project Unify program</a>. The program mixes sports and education to engage students with and without disabilities in athletics and leadership opportunities. Students with disabilities and those without are partnered so that they learn lessons from each other about life on and off the field.</p><div id="module-position-O-zXv6R9N9g" class="story-asset pullquote-asset"><aside class="pull-quote"><p class="pull-quote-item">&ldquo;We want our students to have that social inclusion, that’s our whole goal.&rdquo;</p><span class="pull-quote-attribution">Isaac Sanft, Arizona Unified Sports program director for Special Olympics Arizona</span></aside></div><p>The program also includes opportunities for students who prefer drama or arts over sports. Special Olympics executives say the idea is to create a culture on campus where students in special education programs are as much a part of the school as students on the cheerleading, basketball or other athletic teams.</p><p>“We want our students to have that social inclusion, that’s our whole goal,” says Isaac Sanft, a director with Special Olympics Arizona who heads the organization's <a href="http://www.specialolympicsarizona.org/what-we-do/unified">Arizona Unified Sports</a> program. “And we see that barrier, we see where individuals with disabilities are not included.”</p><p>Sanft says the first time he walks onto an Arizona school campus, before he meets with officials to discuss launching a Project Unify program, he often tries to gauge how inclusive the school is. He starts by asking students and adults on campus if they can direct him to the school’s special-education area. He often gets the same answer.</p><p>“I’ll hear, ‘I don’t know,’” he says. “And those few who do know, they can’t name one special-education student and they don’t know who those (special-education) teachers are.”</p><p>Sanft calls these the invisible barriers that leave children with disabilities out of the traditional high-school experience.</p><h2 class="presto-h2">'The great equalizer'</h2><p>In 2011, Special Olympics Arizona took a major step in breaking down barriers.</p><p>That’s when the organization partnered with the Arizona Interscholastic Association to sanction the Unified Sports program. Now, students who play on Unified Sports teams have the opportunity to earn school letters for athletics.</p><p>Sanft likes to boast about students who have earned their letterman jackets.</p><p>“We use sports as the great equalizer,” Sanft says. “It’s about seeing the abilities in everyone.”</p><p>A few days before the big game, Sanft is making preparations for the visitors traveling to the school. He’s also prepping for a trip to Europe, where he will talk about Project Unify in Arizona.</p><div id="module-position-O-zXv6QYCQU" class="story-asset gallery-asset"><div class="card-film inactive"></div><div class="sidebar-gallery-btn active"></div><div class="companion-story-gallery"><div class="gallery-sidebar-panel"><span class="gallery-sidebar-close"></span><div class="gallery-sidebar-toolbox share-pane social-pane "><span class="util-bar-flyout-nav-btn-wrap"><span

It’s not every day, he says, an Arizona school program for students with disabilities gets national and international attention in the same month.</p><p>Last summer, Sanft and his team at Special Olympics Arizona were discussing the growing success of Arizona’s Unified Sports program. They wanted more people to know the changes happening at schools like Kellis.</p><p>What Sanft says he really wanted was to see a Project Unify at every school in Arizona, and then at every school in the world. Sanft likes to think big.</p><p>But even non-profits with a global reach struggle to make changes at the pace they want, he says. So his team came up with the idea of applying to present at the Zero Project conference, an international gathering that spotlights the best programs and policies for improving the lives of people with disabilities.</p><p>The Zero Project is an initiative launched by the Austria-based Essl Foundation, a charity that funds programs for people in need. Each year, the Zero Project receives hundreds of applications from across the globe to present at the conference at the United Nations headquarters in Austria. Judges select 100 model programs that “improve the daily lives and legal rights of all persons with disabilities,” according to the organization’s website.</p><p>An estimated 500 world policymakers, leaders and diplomats will gather the second week of February at the conference as researchers, educators and others share their work. Sanft will take the stage as one of two Special Olympics Arizona workers who will speak about Project Unify and its athletes.</p><p>“I’m excited,” he says.</p><p>He’s also nervous. But for now, he’s focusing on the rally and the big game at Kellis.</p><h2 class="presto-h2">'We are able'</h2><p>On this particular Tuesday, Sanft is among the hundreds of people at Kellis who look a little stunned by the students who are shouting for each other, exchanging hugs and high fives and comforting those who start to cry.</p><p>The tears come as parents and athletes, with and without disabilities, take the stage to talk about what Unified Sports means to them.</p><p>In the auditorium, Unified Sports athletes are wearing their uniforms. Other students and teachers are wearing T-shirts in different colors with the same message: “We are able.”</p><div id="module-position-O-zXv6RSVvg" class="story-asset embed-asset"><div class="embed"><link rel="stylesheet" type="text/css" href="http://archive.azcentral.com/ic/news/projects/css/linkset.css" /><div class="more-container"><h2>Special Olympics Arizona</h2><p>For more on Special Olympics Arizona and its programs, visit: <a href="http://www.specialolympicsarizona.org/">specialolympicsarizona.org</a>.</p><p>For more on the Zero Project visit: <a href="http://zeroproject.org/conference-2016/">http://zeroproject.org/conference-2016/</a>.</p></div></div></div><p>Students at Kelllis know their Unified Sports program has quietly built a national reputation for their school in the western suburbs of metro Phoenix. That reputation has attracted Tuesday’s visitors from across the United States for a pep rally and the game between Kellis’ Unified basketball team and neighboring Sunrise Mountain High School’s Unified team.</p><p>Seated in the Kellis auditorium are educators, coaches, CEOs and Special Olympics executives. They are there to learn more about the Special Olympics Unified Sports program and to gauge whether it will work at their community's school.</p><p>In a seat at the south end of the auditorium is Timothy Shriver, chairman of Special Olympics. The group was founded in 1968 by his mother, Eunice Kennedy Shriver, and has grown from a few hundred athletes to more than 4.5 million athletes in 170 countries.</p><p>Kellis principal Jeff Wooten asks the students to clap for their guests and for each other. He asks everyone to look under their seats. People reach down and pick up notes on papers about the size of index cards.</p><p>“Those are pledges from last year to eliminate the R-word,” he says, describing the school's effort to stop young people from using a certain slur.</p><p>On one yellow card at a seat near the back of the room it says:</p><p><em>I pledge and support the elimination of the derogatory use of the r-word from everyday speech and promote the acceptance and inclusion of people with intellectual disabilities.</em></p><p>That card is signed in black ink by a student named Vanessa.</p><p>When Wakeford, the teacher who runs Kellis’ Unified Program, takes the stage, students scream for him like he’s their favorite YouTube star.</p><p>“Wakeford! Wakeford!”</p><div id="module-position-O-zXv6RRY2g" class="story-asset image-asset"><aside itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject" class="wide single-photo"><img itemprop="url" src="http://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/983253e1bb9b310ee58102be601351d40cdb2347/c=62-0-3468-2561&amp;r=x408&amp;c=540x405/local/-/media/2016/02/08/Phoenix/Phoenix/635905315388384153--01W1119.jpg" alt="Chris Ensslin, a Raymond S. Parents whose students have graduated from the program talk about the changes they saw in their children. A couple of students with disabilities who graduated show off their navy-blue letterman jackets. One yells: “Cougar Pride, is it in you!”</p><p>And then, there’s Phillipo Howery, a current basketball star on the Kellis Unified Sports team. Wakeford teases Phillipo as he makes his way to the stage.</p><p>“And I know he’s not nervous about the basketball game, but he might be a little nervous right now, huh?”</p><p>“You got this Phillipo!” someone shouts.</p><h2 class="presto-h2">'It has changed my life'</h2><p>Phillipo struts across the stage wearing white sneakers and his navy-blue basketball uniform. He’s holding a couple of sheets of paper.</p><p>Wakeford pats him on the arm. Phillipo grins and sets his papers, the ones that he wrote his speech on, atop the podium.</p><p>Wakeford was wrong. Phillipo is not nervous. He’s been practicing for the crowd of executives, athletes and his peers. And with Noah Enriquez, Phillipo’s partner in Unified Sports.</p><p>Phillipo has something to say about people with disabilities, about Noah and about basketball. It’s his turn to speak.</p><div id="module-position-O-zXv6R6OUg" class="story-asset pullquote-asset"><aside class="pull-quote"><p class="pull-quote-item">&ldquo;After being in Unified Sports, I felt like more confidence in myself because I felt acceptance.&rdquo;</p><span class="pull-quote-attribution">Phillipo Howery, Kellis High School student with disabilities and Unified Program athlete</span></aside></div><p>“Good morning, everybody. My name is Phillipo Howery. I would like to start by saying thank you to you all for being here today. I felt honored to be given the opportunity to speak about Unified Sports — it has changed my life,” he says.</p><p>Phillipo pauses to look at his papers, as if to make sure he’s got the words right.</p><p>“I am very good and competitive at sports and I want to be challenged by other athletes. My favorite sports are basketball first, football next and track last.”</p><p>Phillipo thanks his Unified Sports partner and cracks a joke about Noah looking like Justin Bieber and wrestling like John Cena. (Noah wrestles for Kellis' Varsity wrestling team.) Everyone laughs.</p><p>“Noah taught me how to throw a football … and before that, I taught him a few things on the basketball court, as well,” he says.</p><p>He talks about their special handshake. About their friendship. And then about the sports program that focuses on his abilities.</p><p>“After being in Unified Sports, I felt like more confidence in myself because I felt acceptance,” he says.</p><p>He picks up his papers and walks past the podium and past two signs posted for <em>everyone</em> to see.</p><p>One says: “Everyone is special. Everyone is different.” The other is just a symbol: The letter “R” crossed out.</p><div id="module-position-O-zXv6R8ee4" class="story-asset pullquote-asset"><aside class="pull-quote"><p class="pull-quote-item">&ldquo;It is possible to live in a place where people are valued for who they are. It is possible to teach children to grow up so that they look at each other with love and respect. It is possible to grow up and discover that your best self is the self you give away.&rdquo;</p><span class="pull-quote-attribution">Timothy Shriver, Special Olympics chairman</span></aside></div><p>The principal is about to introduce the school’s Unified dance team members, who will close out the rally. But first he asks Shriver, the international Special Olympics leader, if he’d like to say a few words.</p><p>Shriver thanks the students, the educators, the coaches and the CEOs, who came from 30 states for a special rally and the big game. He asks them to think about what they are seeing, and taking part in, at Kellis High in Arizona:</p><p>“That it is possible to live in a place where people are valued for who they are. It is possible to teach children to grow up so that they look at each other with love and respect. It is possible to grow up and discover that your best self is the self you give away. <em>It is possible</em>.”</p><p>As the crowd cheers, the athletes head to the gym to get ready for the game. Phillipo knows it’s almost time to play basketball.</p><p>He’s ready. He’s been waiting.</p><p>When he’s on the court his partners pass him the ball. He makes more baskets than he misses. His classmates in the bleachers scream each time either team makes a shot.</p><p>When the buzzer sounds, and the game is over, no one pays attention to the score. Everyone in the bleachers rushes the court to rally for the athletes.</p><p>Phillipo is out of breath. A reporter asks him about the shots he made and what he thinks was his best run on the court.</p><p>He nods his head, smiles and says, “My favorite moment was to have fun."</p><p>A teammate walks up next to him. He wraps his arm around Phillipo. They walk off the court, together.</p><div id="module-position-O-zXv6RMFwo" class="story-asset image-asset"><aside itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject" class="wide single-photo"><img itemprop="url" src="http://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/051b309d78afd278442a0161f11cefc22009ec4b/c=136-0-3194-2299&amp;r=x408&amp;c=540x405/local/-/media/2016/02/05/Phoenix/Phoenix/635902936762719374--01W1359.jpg" alt="Ryan Silver, (center) a Raymond S.
