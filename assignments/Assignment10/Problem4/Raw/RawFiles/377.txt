		<p>GRAND RAPIDS, MI  Grand Rapids Public Schools and the Student Advancement Foundation announced the launch of a community-wide fundraiser Tuesday, Feb. 9, designed to keep offering sports to students for free. </p>

<p>The campaign, announced in the gym of the Martin Luther King Leadership Academy, will support a <a href="http://www.grsaf.org/gr8-sports-great-kids/">GR8 Sports, Great Kids</a> effort. The youth sports program ensures access to kindergarten through eighth-grade sports to all students, regardless of their families' ability to pay. </p>

<p>Funding for GR8 Sports comes from a combination of community support and GRPS. By the 2018-19 school year, officials say community support will need to be at least $500,000 annually. GRPS would contribute $300,000.</p>

<p>"So many school programs require students to 'pay to play' but in Grand Rapids we said that's not what we wanted," Superintendent Teresa Weatherall Neal told the parents, philanthropists and community leaders who gathered for the event.</p>

<p>"The students' access to physical activities is critical to enhance their health and wellness. This impacts their ability to go to school, to focus, to study and ultimately, to learn.''</p>

<p>SAF was created in 1993 to provide a channel for the community to contribute supplemental funds for GRPS activities and programs. </p>

<p>Michele Suchovsky, executive director of the Grand Rapids Student Advancement Foundation, said for seven years the organization has worked with GRPS, first with providing funding for elementary sports, and later joining the Doug and Maria DeVos Foundation in supporting middle school programming.  </p>

<p>Suchovsky said a year ago, they formally named the initiative GR8 Sports, Great Kids, and decided to hold a fundraiser to get the community invested to sustain and grow the program.</p>

<p>"We use sports to empower kids to learn, develop and thrive,'' she said.  </p>

<div id="asset-19712636" class="entry_widget_large entry_widget_right"><span class="adv-photo-large"><a href="http://media.mlive.com/grpress/news_impact/photo/triojpg-9e0798ff27c866a8.jpg" target="_blank" class="full-size-popup"><img src="/static/common/img/blank.gif" class="lazy adv-photo" data-position="article-large" data-original="http://media.mlive.com/grpress/news_impact/photo/19712636-large.jpg" alt="trio.jpg" /></a><span class="photo-data"><span class="caption">Photo of Grand Rapids University Preparatory seventh-grade student athletes. From (L to R) Dahlia Mendez, Ryann Rhodes and Tamya Mendez. The students play multiple sports and maintain a 4.0 GPA. </span><span class="byline">Monica Scott  MLive.com</span></span></span>&nbsp;</div>

<p>This school year, there are around 5,000 students engaged in 14 after-school sports programs. </p>

<p>Shay and Julio Mendez, who have five children at Gerald R. Ford Academic Center and Grand Rapids University Preparatory Academy (UPrep), said it would be very expensive if they had to pay for their children to participate in athletics. </p>

<p>"Our children have always been really good in school but sports pushes them even more and gives them more of an opportunity for scholarships for both academics and sports," said Shay Mendez. "I am appreciative of the contributions that allow our kids to participate for free."</p>

<p>The couple said they were moved by the remarks from Doug DeVos, co-founder of GR8 Sports, Great Kids.</p>

<p>"When you put other activities into a child's life and you connect the community in a way such as this, you create opportunities," said DeVos, who said cost can be a huge barrier to students having a meaningful experience in life. </p>

<p>DeVos told the students present they are worthy of investment and attention. He said the sky's the limit, if they are willing to work hard and challenge themselves.</p>

<p>"You are vital," he said. "As a community we have high expectations for you. We want to create opportunities and eliminate barriers."</p>

<p>The Mendezes' daughter, Dahlia Mendez, a seventh-grader at UPrep who plays multiple sports, addressed the crowd after DeVos. The city wrestling and cross country champion said sports are fun and teach teamwork and leadership skills.</p>

<p>"I love sports because you get to bond with people," said Dahlia, a 4.0 student. <br />
"The people you meet can become your best friends for life."</p>

<p>K-5 sports currently offered in the program include: soccer, track and field, basketball and cross country. Sports for those in grades 6-8 include: co-ed cross country, boys football, girls volleyball, boys and girls soccer, boys and girls basketball, boys wrestling, co-ed bowling, boys baseball, girls softball, co-ed competitive cheer, co-ed track and co-ed swim.</p>

<p>Jolinda Lucas, athletic director for U Prep/ Grand Rapids Montessori/Museum School, said 43 percent (approximately 80 students) of UPrep's students participate in sports and the students involved average over a 3.0 GPA. </p>

<p>Funding is also being sought for GR8 Girls, a subset of GR8 Sports, Great Kids. </p>

<p>"Our goal is to positively promote and support girls' athletics within GRPS," said Cara Jones, director of GR8 Sports, Great Kids. "Just in the last year more than 2,000 girls participated in the program and we were able to provide the equipment, uniforms as well as coaching and transportation needs."</p>

<p>Program officials are looking for volunteer coaches for all K-8 sports. They need coaches who can commit during the season to one or two-hour-long practices each week and a game or meet on Saturday.  </p>

<p>Those interested in the donating to the program or becoming a volunteer can find more information about GR8, Great Kids <a href="http://www.grsaf.org/gr8-sports-great-kids/">online </a>or by calling SAF at 616-988-5430.</p>
